Good morning. My capstone is about a measurement problem that appears the moment people start trading on a forecast. The two lines behind the title are the finding itself, drawn from the actual simulation: the same forecast, on the same market paths, scored two ways. One reading climbs while the other decays. In the next fifteen minutes I will show you why both are true at once, and what that means for anyone who evaluates a deployed forecast.
Act I · The problem

When a forecast gains users, its success starts to move its own target

→

What exactly changed?

The returns now carry the forecast's own fingerprint. The score moved, but the reading is no longer innocent.

The forecast's skill, the market, or the meaning of the measurement?
Start with the story. A forecasting service goes live. At launch nobody trades on it, so its score is honest: it measures found structure. Then it earns subscribers, and their coordinated orders begin to move the very returns used to grade the forecast. When the tracked accuracy now shifts, three readings are all defensible: the model got better, the market changed, or the measurement itself changed meaning. That third possibility is the thesis. My project builds the smallest market in which the three can be told apart exactly.
Act I · The problem · what the field already knows

Three literatures meet here, and none scores a forecast inside the market it moves

Tradition 01

Reflexivity & performative prediction

Views alter the outcomes they are about. Deployment shifts the distribution a model is judged on; accuracy becomes a property of model plus behavioural response.

Soros 1987 · Perdomo et al. 2020
Tradition 02

Heterogeneous-agent finance

Mean-variance traders switch between competing rules; a market maker moves the price linearly in net order flow. Supplies a market where trading feeds back into returns.

Brock & Hommes 1998 · Beja & Goldman 1980 · Farmer & Joshi 2002
Tradition 03

Out-of-sample forecast evaluation

Fit through t, forecast t+1, summarise as out-of-sample R². The machinery presumes a target the forecast does not move.

Diebold & Mariano 1995
Adoption breaks exactly the premise the evaluation tradition rests on. That intersection is unoccupied.
Three research traditions each hold a piece. Soros argued markets are not passive measurement devices; Perdomo and coauthors made that formal as performative prediction: the deployed model shifts the data it is judged on. Heterogeneous-agent finance gives me the market: Brock-Hommes demand, a Beja-Goldman / Farmer-Joshi market maker that moves price in order flow. And classical forecast evaluation gives the scoring machinery, which quietly assumes the target is exogenous. Adoption breaks precisely that assumption. Nobody had put a performative forecast inside such a market and scored it both ways. That is the gap.

Act I · The problem · research question
How does adoption of a shared return forecast change its measured accuracy, and the independent signal that accuracy is supposed to certify?
Self-fulfilling channel ↗

Accuracy against the realised return: the only reading a deployed forecaster can observe. Expected to rise with adoption.

Erosion channel ↘

Accuracy against the demand-adjusted counterfactual: the path stripped of the users' own price impact. Expected to fall.

So the research question, precisely: when a shared autoregressive forecast is adopted by more and more traders, what happens to its accuracy against the return the market actually prints, and what happens to its accuracy against the counterfactual return with the users' own price impact stripped out? Two named channels, with opposite expected signs, on the same path. Everything that follows is the machinery to measure them and the result of doing so.
Act II · The laboratory · the market

One equation carries the whole mechanism

rt+1 = φ rt + μ Dt + σ εt+1
φ r : the signal

A small persistent component, φ = 0.25 at baseline. This is the structure the forecasting rule is built to recover.

μ D : the feedback door

A market maker absorbs aggregate demand and moves the quote. The only channel through which trading re-enters returns.

σ ε : the news

Exogenous Gaussian shocks. Unpredictable by construction; no forecast earns anything here.

submit signed orders qᵢ each period

Market maker

absorbs mean demand D, shifts log-price by μD

Return realises

signal + impact + news; next period repeats

N = 200T = 8,000μ = 0.05φ = 0.25σ = 0.01metrics on trailing 1,000
The market is deliberately minimal. One asset. Each period, two hundred traders submit orders; a risk-neutral market maker absorbs the average order and shifts the log-price in proportion, coefficient mu. The resulting return law has three terms, and the whole talk lives in their colours. Amber: a small autoregressive signal, phi times last return, the honest structure. Blue: mu times demand, the feedback door, the only way trading touches returns. Grey: news, unpredictable by anyone. Everything reflexive that happens in this market walks through that one blue term.
Act II · The laboratory · traders and adoption

Two rules trade; adoption is the dial we turn

Non-adopters · the null rule

Active but skill-free

Zero-mean random orders: they keep the market trading and inject directionally uninformative flow. The benchmark any real signal must beat.

Adopters · the shared rule

One public AR(1) forecast

Rolling least-squares on the last w = 250 returns, strictly out of sample. Mean-variance position, capped: adopters become fixed-size traders on the forecast's sign. Every adopter sees the same number.

How adoption spreads
Mechanism A · exogenous

Stochastic diffusion

Each non-adopter discovers the rule with probability π per period. Adoption is imposed, isolating the pure effect of wider use.

Mechanism B · endogenous

Performance-based switching

Entry follows recent risk-adjusted profit of the rule versus the null (logistic in the certainty-equivalent gap). Early success recruits, recruits reshape returns.

Trader behaviour has two states. Non-adopters trade random zero-mean orders: active, but skill-free, the right benchmark. Adopters all read the same public rolling AR(1) forecast, fitted on the last 250 returns, strictly out of sample, and size it mean-variance with a cap that binds nearly always, so in practice an adopter is a fixed-size trade on the forecast's sign. Because the rule is public, adopter flow is coordinated: it does not average out. Adoption itself moves two ways: an exogenous diffusion, my clean experimental dial, and an endogenous rule where recent risk-adjusted success recruits new adopters. That second one closes the full reflexive loop.

Scoreboard 2 · demand-adjusted

Did the independent signal survive?

R²da : forecast vs xt+1 = rt+1 − μDt

A counterfactual: the path had adopters not moved today's quote. Keeps the signal, strips the self-fulfilment.

Here is the measurement trick the whole project turns on. The realised return decomposes exactly: signal, plus the adopters' contemporaneous impact, plus news. So I score the same forecast twice on the same path. Scoreboard one: against the realised return, impact included; that is all a deployed forecaster can ever compute. Scoreboard two: against the demand-adjusted counterfactual, the same period with the blue block removed; did the independent signal the rule was built to find actually survive? One caveat I state precisely: this strips only contemporaneous impact, it is not the regression residual. It answers: what would the forecast have scored had its users not moved today's quote.
Act II · The laboratory · controls before claims

The laboratory is clean before the feedback valve opens

Before any claim about adoption, three controls. First, the baseline market with only null traders is well behaved: near-Gaussian returns, and the measured autocorrelation matches the phi I put in. Second, and this underpins everything: across one hundred seeds, the rolling effective phi does not drift; total range under two hundredths over four thousand periods. So if that coefficient later moves, adoption did it. Third, the forecasting rule works when nobody trades it: scored offline it recovers a small, positive, stable out-of-sample R-squared, approaching the theoretical ceiling of phi squared as the window grows. A real but modest signal. Now I open the valve.

Every adopter pushes the same way

Coordinated orders move the realised return in the forecast's direction, so the forecast partly scores against its own price impact.

Monotone in adoption, not in time

Channel one. On the horizontal axis, the fraction of traders using the forecast; vertical, its rolling out-of-sample R-squared against realised returns, pooled over one hundred seeds. It climbs, roughly monotonically, and on the paired single path it goes 0.07 at zero adoption to 0.19 near full adoption: measured accuracy nearly triples. The mechanism is banal once you see it: every adopter trades the same public signal, their block of orders pushes the return in the forecast's direction, and the score partly grades the forecast on a move it made itself. The control line on identical shocks does not move, so this is adoption, not time.

Half the signal's content, gone

Once self-fulfilment is removed from the target, the forecast's grip on the surviving structure weakens as adoption rises.

The realised score hides this

Improvement and decay arrive on the same path, from the same order flow. The deployed metric shows only the first.

Channel two, and this is the punchline of the project. Same forecasts, same paths, same seeds; the only change is the target: I strip the adopters' contemporaneous impact back out. Now the reading falls as adoption rises, 0.08 to 0.04 on the paired run. Half the independent content is gone by full adoption. Hold both slides together: the same order flow that inflates the realised score erodes the signal underneath it. A deployed forecaster watching the realised number would celebrate at exactly the moment their edge is halving. The score cannot see the difference; that is the measurement problem made concrete.
Act III · The result · why it happens

Adopters print extra persistence into the market, and the rolling fit swallows it

1 · Forecast carries yesterday's sign

An AR(1) forecast is affine in the last return, so adopters trade the sign of rₜ.

2 · Impact reinforces it

Their block μDₜ pushes the next return the same way: manufactured autocorrelation, 0.28 → 0.45.

3 · The fit calibrates to the echo

The rolling regression absorbs the amplified coefficient and over-predicts the counterfactual, whose true φ never moved. That over-prediction is the erosion.

Absorption does not damp it

Market-maker absorption cannot offset it: μD adds to the next return, it does not revert it.

Why does this happen? Three chained steps. One: an AR(1) forecast is basically yesterday's return times a positive slope, so adopters trade yesterday's sign. Two: their coordinated block enters the next return with a positive sign, which is manufactured autocorrelation; the measured persistence climbs from 0.28 toward 0.45, well above the true 0.25 that never changes. Three: the rolling regression, doing its job, calibrates to that amplified echo and now systematically over-shoots the counterfactual process, whose persistence is still 0.25. That over-prediction is the erosion channel. Note the counterintuitive part: market-maker absorption does not damp this; the impact term adds, it does not mean-revert.
Act III · The result · stress-testing the finding

Not a transient, and not an artefact of how adoption spreads

Two stress tests. Left: I extend the run to thirty thousand periods, fifty seeds, with the market at or near full adoption for roughly twenty-five thousand of them. The two readings hold their separated levels indefinitely, realised near 0.17, demand-adjusted near 0.02. This is a steady state of the saturated market, not a transient of the adoption ramp. Right: replace my imposed diffusion with the endogenous performance-based rule, on identical shocks. The adoption path changes shape completely; the erosion does not. At matched shares both mechanisms give the same demand-adjusted decline. Erosion is a property of the adoption share, however it is generated.

3,600 runs map where the signal loses half its content

Threshold reached
67/90
AR(1) cells · mean detected share 0.29

Stronger signals survive longer

The crossing arrives later as φ grows: from near-zero shares at φ = 0.10 to 0.37–0.65 at φ = 0.30.

Impact deepens the hole

At the strongest price impact the tail demand-adjusted R² is pushed below zero (to −0.28): past halving, to worse than useless.

Survives the forecaster's complexity

Same pattern at AR order p = 1, 2, 5, 10 (67/59/42/28 cells); nowhere does it reverse. The realised score never systematically erodes anywhere.

Is this corner-of-parameter-space luck? The sweep says no: five price impacts, six persistence levels, three windows, four AR orders, ten seeds each, thirty-six hundred runs. The map shows the adoption share at which the independent signal has lost half its own baseline. It fires in 67 of 90 AR(1) cells, on average at 29 percent adoption, which is uncomfortably early. Two regularities organise it: stronger true signals take more adoption to halve, and stronger price impact deepens the erosion, pushing the tail reading below zero at the extreme. The pattern survives every AR order tested. And the realised-return score never systematically erodes anywhere: its rare threshold hits are noise crossings at near-zero baselines: the designed non-result.
Act IV · So what · implications

Once a forecast has users, realised accuracy stops measuring skill alone

For anyone deploying forecasts
  • Realised R² over-reports under adoption: it conflates found signal with the users' own price impact, and improves exactly while the content erodes.
  • Report both readings. The gap between them is the deployment-driven share of measured accuracy, the quantity the headline number silently absorbs.
  • The audit needs the period's order flow D and impact μ: precisely what an external evaluator usually lacks. That is a data-access argument, not a technicality.
For the theory
  • Performative prediction's split of accuracy into a stable part and a deployment-driven part becomes measurable inside a market with explicit price impact.
  • The demand-adjusted target identifies the stable component; the threshold maps chart how much adoption moves each diagnostic by a fixed fraction.
  • Realised evaluation cannot distinguish a forecast that predicts the market from one the market has come to obey.
What should you take from this? If you deploy forecasts: the realised score, the only one you can compute, over-reports once your users have price impact, and it improves precisely while your edge decays. So report both readings where you can; their gap is the deployment-driven component of your accuracy. And notice the audit requires the period's order flow and the impact coefficient, exactly what outsiders lack: there is a data-access argument buried here. For theory: Perdomo's split between a model's stable accuracy and its deployment effect stops being conceptual; in a market with explicit impact, it is a number you can compute. The one-line version: realised evaluation cannot tell a forecast that predicts the market from one the market has come to obey.
Act IV · So what · boundaries and takeaway

Scope, by design

Single asset · linear price impact · AR(p) forecasters only · homogeneous null pool. The smallest honest laboratory, not a calibrated market.

Where the mechanism needs signal

At φ ≤ 0.10 the baseline is near zero: little signal to lose. Amplification needs both φ and μ high enough to bite.

Deferred, honestly

The economic endpoint (transaction costs, A*profit) was attempted; a position-size asymmetry between null and adopter rules must be neutralised first. Future work.

A market that obeys its forecast will grade that forecast generously. Audit it against the path it did not cause.

Backup · intra-period timing

The order of events makes the decomposition well defined

1

Agents observe returns up to and including t and form the shared forecast r̂t+1.

2

Adopters and non-adopters submit orders qi,t.

3

The market maker absorbs aggregate demand and moves the quote immediately: μDt is in the books.

4

The news shock σεt+1 and the autoregressive term φrt realise.

The forecast knows everything through t before any order is placed; the impact of step three is booked before the news of step four arrives. That strict sequence is what makes scoring one forecast against both the realised return and the impact-stripped counterfactual well defined on the same path.

The realised-channel threshold existing but firing only as noise is itself the designed non-result: realised accuracy does not erode.
