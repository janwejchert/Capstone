{\Large\bfseries\color{accent} Abstract\par}
\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{minipage}{0.86\textwidth}
\justifying\small
This report studies how widespread adoption of a shared autoregressive forecasting rule affects forecast performance and economic value in a single-asset agent-based market. The market combines Brock-Hommes mean-variance demand with a Beja-Goldman / Farmer-Joshi market-maker price update. Traders may switch from a no-skill null rule to a public rolling AR forecast under either exogenous stochastic diffusion or endogenous certainty-equivalent switching. The forecast is evaluated against two distinct targets on the same path: the realised return, which is the metric a deployed forecaster actually sees, and a demand-adjusted counterfactual that strips out contemporaneous price impact.

The simulator identifies a dual-channel result. As adoption rises, the rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ against realised returns reaches roughly 0.19 at full adoption, against 0.07 for a zero-adoption control on the same shocks, through coordinated adopter demand. The same forecast's $R^{2}$ against the demand-adjusted return falls to roughly 0.04, against 0.08 under the control, with the rolling effective AR coefficient rising from 0.28 to 0.45 in step. Mean adopter profit is amplified rather than eroded in the cost-free baseline; the null-relative economic comparison is dominated by position-size asymmetry between the null rule and the capped adopter rule and is reported with that caveat. The headline summary is therefore that adoption raises realised predictive performance through a self-fulfilment channel while reducing the independent demand-adjusted signal the forecast was originally designed to exploit.
\end{minipage}

\section{The model}\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Single-asset market with linear price impact}\label{sec:model-market}

The market is deliberately minimal. Time is discrete and indexed by $t = 0, 1, \ldots, T$. A single risky asset trades at log-price $p_t$, with one-period return $r_{t+1} = p_{t+1} - p_t$; the risk-free rate is normalised to zero and the asset pays no dividend, so $r_{t+1}$ is an excess return. There are $N$ traders, each submitting a signed order $q_{i,t}$ every period. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.1 (asset and agents)
There is no Walrasian auctioneer clearing supply against demand. Price formation instead follows the disequilibrium market-adjustment tradition, in which a risk-neutral market maker absorbs whatever aggregate order flow arrives and shifts the log-price in proportion to it \citep{beja1980,farmerjoshi2002}. Linear price impact is the single channel through which trading feeds back into returns, and therefore the channel that carries every reflexive effect this report studies. The three equations below define the market side of the model; trader-level order formation is the subject of the next subsection. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1 (Baseline market environment), eqs. (4)-(6)

Aggregate signed demand is the per-trader average order,
\begin{equation}
  D_t \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} q_{i,t},
  \label{eq:aggregate-demand}
\end{equation}
and the market maker updates the log-price according to
\begin{equation}
  p_{t+1} \;=\; p_t \;+\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad \varepsilon_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1),
  \label{eq:price-update}
\end{equation}
where $\mu = 1/\lambda > 0$ is the market-maker price-impact coefficient, the reciprocal of the market maker's liquidity $\lambda$, and $\sigma > 0$ scales the exogenous news shock; equation~\eqref{eq:price-update} is the zero-fundamental special case of the Farmer-Joshi log-linear price update. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.4 (eqs. 4 and 5; "zero-fundamental specialisation of Farmer and Joshi (2002, Eq. 7)"); liquidity reading of lambda per farmerjoshi2002 (discussion of their eq. 7)
First-differencing \eqref{eq:price-update} gives a one-period market-maker return of $\mu D_t + \sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$; the baseline return law augments it with an autoregressive component carried in from prior periods,
\begin{equation}
  r_{t+1} \;=\; \varphi\, r_t \;+\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad |\varphi| < 1\ \text{small.}
  \label{eq:return-law}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:return-law} splits the return into the three pieces the rest of the model works with: a small predictable component $\varphi r_t$, the signal the rolling AR rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} is built to estimate; the contemporaneous price impact $\mu D_t$, the only term through which trader behaviour enters; and unpredictable news $\sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$. The $\varphi r_t$ term is not ad hoc: \citet{farmerjoshi2002} show analytically that when part of aggregate demand comes from linear trend-following strategies, the induced return process has positive first-lag autocorrelation of the same order as the trend-following intensity, and \eqref{eq:return-law} absorbs that component as a single reduced-form coefficient rather than modelling a separate trader type at baseline. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 6; Farmer and Joshi (2002, Eqs. 8 to 11), rho_r(1) = alpha/(1+alpha))

One implementation choice deserves note. In \citet{farmerjoshi2002} the market maker absorbs net order flow, that is, changes in positions; the simulator's \texttt{market.py} has it absorb the level of aggregate demand each period, closer in spirit to the Beja-Goldman excess-demand adjustment, so a persistent position exerts persistent price pressure. The profit and transaction-cost accounting of later phases prices a full position turnover every period, consistent with this convention. % src: src/reflexive_market/market.py module docstring

\subsection{Trader behaviour: belief, decision, and switching layers}\label{sec:model-traders}

Trader behaviour decomposes into three layers, and keeping them separate is what later allows the results to say where each channel of the dual-channel result is produced: a belief layer (what return the agent expects), a decision layer (how that belief becomes a position), and a switching layer (whether the agent uses the shared rule at all). The mechanism under study operates on the belief layer, where the rolling AR forecast is public information, and transmits to returns only through the decision and switching layers, where action is private. At the belief layer there are exactly two states. An adopter's belief is the shared rolling autoregressive forecast $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ of section~\ref{sec:model-rules}, identical across adopters because the rule is public. A non-adopter holds no belief about returns at all: the null rule of the same section generates order flow that is directionally uninformative on average, an active but skill-free benchmark rather than a behavioural model. % src: proposal v4 section 3.2 (A three-layer model of trader behaviour); section 3.3 (null rule, "directionally uninformative on average")

The decision layer follows \citet{brockhommes1998}: each trader is a myopic mean-variance maximiser with constant absolute risk aversion. The two equations below give the demand mapping and the capped adopter order; the order-combination rule and the profit definition appear inline in the next paragraph. With conditional forecast $\hat{r}_{i,t+1}$ of the next-period return and common perceived conditional variance $\sigma^{2} > 0$, the optimal signed position is % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.2 (Trader demand, eq. 1)
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,t} \;=\; \frac{\hat{r}_{i,t+1}}{a\, \sigma^{2}},
  \label{eq:bh-demand}
\end{equation}
where $a > 0$ is the coefficient of absolute risk aversion; this is the zero-dividend, zero-rate special case of the Brock-Hommes demand function. A trader buys when the expected return is positive, sells when it is negative, and submits smaller positions when the market is perceived as riskier. Adopters evaluate \eqref{eq:bh-demand} at the public forecast rather than at an idiosyncratic belief, with the result clipped at a position cap, % src: proposal v4 sections 3.1.2 (economic meaning of eq. 1) and 3.1.3 (eq. 3)
\begin{equation}
  q_{i,t}^{(A)} \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}}{a\, \sigma^{2}},\; -\bar{q}_{i},\; +\bar{q}_{i}\right),
  \label{eq:advanced-order}
\end{equation}
where the cap $\bar{q}_{i} > 0$ acts as a leverage or inventory constraint: rolling-window estimates of $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ are noisy, and the cap prevents a single extreme forecast from generating an unboundedly large trade. Non-adopters bypass the demand mapping entirely; their null order enters as drawn. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.3 (role of the position cap)

The switching layer is a single binary state: $a_{i,t} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates whether trader $i$ has adopted the shared rule at time $t$. The final order combines the two rules as $q_{i,t} = (1 - a_{i,t})\, q_{i,t}^{(0)} + a_{i,t}\, q_{i,t}^{(A)}$, and the one-period profit that feeds both the economic endpoint and the performance-based switching rule is $\Pi_{i,t+1} = q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$. How $a_{i,t}$ evolves, by stochastic diffusion or by certainty-equivalent comparison, is the subject of section~\ref{sec:model-adoption}. Two implementation details from \texttt{traders.py} are worth recording. First, only the product $a\sigma^{2}$ enters the demand mapping, so the simulator passes it as a single risk-scale parameter rather than fixing $a$ and the perceived variance separately. Second, the advanced order returns zero whenever the forecast is not finite, so the warm-up periods before the first rolling window fills leave adopters effectively inactive instead of injecting undefined demand. % src: proposal v4 sections 3.1.3 (eq. 2) and 3.1.6 (eq. 8); src/reflexive_market/traders.py advanced_order docstring (risk_scale, non-finite forecast guard); src/reflexive_market/simulate.py main loop (orders = (1 - adoption) * null + adoption * q_adv)

\subsection{Null rule and rolling AR forecasting rule}\label{sec:model-rules}

Non-adopters trade by the null rule: trader $i$ submits the order $q_{i,t}^{(0)} = \xi_{i,t}$ with $\xi_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{q}^{2})$, drawn independently across traders and time. The rule is deliberately a reference point rather than a model of real trading. It keeps every trader active every period while injecting order flow that is zero mean and directionally uninformative on average, so any systematic predictability or profitability the advanced rule generates is measured against a market in which trading happens but is not forecast-driven. One design feature matters downstream: the null order bypasses the demand mapping of the previous subsection and is subject to no position cap. It enters aggregate demand as drawn, with typical magnitude set by $\sigma_q$ alone, while the advanced order \eqref{eq:advanced-order} is clipped at $\bar{q}_{i}$. This position-size asymmetry between the two rules returns in section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}, where it dominates the null-relative economic endpoint in both directions. % src: proposal v4 section 3.3 (Null model of trading, eq. 9; "no-skill random trading rule", "directionally uninformative on average"); src/reflexive_market/traders.py null_orders (draws returned unclipped; the clip in eq. (3) applies only in advanced_order)

Adopters share a single advanced rule: an autoregressive forecast of the next return, estimated on a rolling window of recent returns. With rolling-window length $w$ and autoregressive order $p$, at time $t$ the public model fits by least squares % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 (Advanced forecasting rule, eqs. 10 and 11)
\begin{equation}
  r_{s+1} \;=\; c \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \varphi_{j}\, r_{s+1-j} \;+\; u_{s+1},
  \label{eq:ar-fit}
\end{equation}
using only the observations in the window $s = t - w, \ldots, t - 1$, and issues the one-step-ahead out-of-sample forecast
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}(w, p) \;=\; \hat{c}_{t, w, p} \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \hat{\varphi}_{j, t, w, p}\, r_{t+1-j}.
  \label{eq:ar-forecast}
\end{equation}
Nothing estimated at time $t$ uses information from after $t$, so the forecast is out of sample by construction, and rolling estimation lets the coefficients adapt when the return process changes, which under adoption it does: the rolling fit of \eqref{eq:ar-fit} is exactly the object that later absorbs the amplified empirical autocorrelation of realised returns. % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 ("using only information that would have been available at time t"; "Rolling estimation allows the coefficients to adapt"); section 4.1.2 (rolling fit picks up an amplified empirical autocorrelation)
The rule is built to recover the $\varphi r_t$ component of the return law \eqref{eq:return-law}, the simplest forecasting environment that should in principle work; penalised regressions, tree ensembles, and recurrent architectures are deliberately excluded. In the simulator, \texttt{forecast.py} implements the fit as ordinary least squares on a lagged design matrix with an intercept, the order parameter defaults to $p = 1$, and the first $w$ entries of the forecast series are NaN, the warm-up that the non-finite-forecast guard of the previous subsection converts into adopter inactivity. The core design holds $p = 1$ with windows $w \in \{50, 100, 250\}$; higher orders $p \in \{2, 5, 10\}$ enter only as a robustness check in phase 6. % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 (purpose of the baseline, excluded model classes, w in {50, 100, 250}, AR(1) core); section 3.7 (higher-order AR rule, p in {2, 5, 10}, phase 6 robustness); src/reflexive_market/forecast.py rolling_ar_forecast (np.linalg.lstsq via fit_ar, p=1 default, first window entries NaN)

\subsection{Adoption mechanisms}\label{sec:model-adoption}

Two mechanisms govern how the adoption indicator $a_{i,t}$ evolves; both act on the switching layer of section~\ref{sec:model-traders} alone, and the simulator runs exactly one at a time. The stochastic mechanism comes first because it is easier to interpret: it asks whether wider use of the forecast is by itself sufficient to change predictability. The performance-based mechanism then studies the feedback between early success and later crowding. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5 ("Two mechanisms are considered ... Both act on the switching layer of section 3.2"; closing paragraph on implementation order); src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (ValueError when diffusion and switching are both active)

\subsubsection{Stochastic diffusion}

Under stochastic diffusion each non-adopter discovers and adopts the public rule with a fixed probability $\pi \in (0, 1)$ per period, and each adopter abandons it with an optional small exit probability $\delta$: % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.1 (Stochastic diffusion, eqs. 12 and 13)
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 1 \mid a_{i,t} = 0) \;=\; \pi,
  \label{eq:diffusion-entry}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 0 \mid a_{i,t} = 1) \;=\; \delta.
  \label{eq:diffusion-exit}
\end{equation}
The draws are independent across agents and periods, so the adoption share $A_t = (1/N)\sum_i a_{i,t}$ moves at an expected per-period rate of $\pi(1 - A_t) - \delta A_t$, with no social learning and no profitability comparison: adoption is imposed on the market rather than generated by it, isolating the pure effect of wider use of a common signal. % src: src/reflexive_market/adoption.py stochastic_diffusion_step (independent per-agent transitions); proposal v4 section 3.5.1 ("without explicitly modelling social learning or profitability comparisons", "isolating the pure effect of wider use of a common signal"); expected rate follows from eqs. (12) and (13) by counting expected entries pi(1-A_t) and exits delta A_t
One implementation detail matters for every later comparison: adoption draws come from a dedicated random generator, so changing $\pi$ or $\delta$ cannot advance the market shock stream, and regimes with different adoption settings run on paired shocks. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (adoption_rng spawn comment: no-op adoption settings leave the realised market path identical; "across-regime comparisons stay paired on the same shocks")

\subsubsection{Certainty-equivalent performance-based switching}

The economically meaningful mechanism makes entry depend on recent relative risk-adjusted success, scored in the same mean-variance terms as the decision layer. Each rule $k \in \{(0), (A)\}$ carries a one-period certainty-equivalent return, % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (Risk-adjusted performance-based switching; "the switching score is constructed to be consistent with that preference structure")
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CE}_t^{(k)} \;=\; \bar{\Pi}_t^{(k)} \;-\; \tfrac{a}{2}\, \hat{\sigma}_{t}^{2,(k)},
  \label{eq:ce}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Pi}_t^{(k)}$ and $\hat{\sigma}_{t}^{2,(k)}$ are the rolling mean and variance of the rule's per-period profit over a window of length $W_A$, and $a$ is the risk-aversion coefficient of the decision layer. The switching score is the gap $S_t = \mathrm{CE}_t^{(A)} - \mathrm{CE}_t^{(0)}$, and each non-adopter switches to the advanced rule with the logistic probability % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 14 and the inline definition of S_t)
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 1 \mid a_{i,t} = 0) \;=\; \Lambda(\alpha + \beta\, S_t), \qquad \Lambda(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}},
  \label{eq:logit-switch}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is a baseline adoption term and $\beta > 0$ controls sensitivity to recent performance; the specification is the binary \citet{brockhommes1998} discrete-choice mechanism applied at the switching layer. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 15 and surrounding prose, "binary BH98 discrete-choice mechanism")
Two implementation choices complete the rule. First, the design specifies an entry probability analogous to \eqref{eq:diffusion-entry} but no exit analogue of \eqref{eq:diffusion-exit}, and \texttt{adoption.py} keeps that asymmetry: adopters retain their state, so the adoption share can only ratchet upward under this mechanism. % src: src/reflexive_market/adoption.py performance_switching_step docstring ("The proposal specifies only the entry direction ... Adopters keep their state. This matches the asymmetric form used by stochastic diffusion") and code (entry-only update, no exit branch); proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 15 conditions on a_{i,t} = 0 only)
Second, the profit streams feeding \eqref{eq:ce} belong to one representative trader per rule: the common capped adopter order, and the realised order of a single representative non-adopter, which keeps the per-trader mean and variance of null profit independent of the adoption share. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (representative-profit comment block; null representative is the lowest-indexed non-adopter)
Unlike stochastic diffusion, this mechanism closes a second reflexive loop: early adopter success raises $\mathrm{CE}_t^{(A)}$ and accelerates entry, and the entrants' own trading reshapes the returns from which every later certainty equivalent is computed. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5 closing paragraph ("feedback between early success and later crowding")

\subsection{Intra-period timing}\label{sec:model-timing}

The equations of the preceding subsections fix what each trader and the market maker do, but not when within a period they do it, and in an agent-based market that order of operations is not implied by the equations alone. The positive-sign price-impact term $\mu D_t$ in the return law \eqref{eq:return-law} is what makes the order load-bearing: if adopter demand formed on today's forecast moves today's quote, then the forecast and its evaluation target are entangled within the period, and the forecast can appear self-fulfilling rather than self-eroding unless the sequence of events is made explicit. The design resolves this by fixing the following sequence inside each period, and the simulator's single state-evolution loop, \texttt{run()} in \texttt{simulate.py}, follows it exactly. % src: proposal v4 section 3.6 (Intra-period timing and the two evaluation channels), opening paragraph; src/reflexive_market/simulate.py module docstring (loop follows the section 3.6 timing)

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
\item Agents observe returns up to and including period $t$ and form forecasts $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ per \eqref{eq:ar-forecast}.
\item Adopters and non-adopters submit orders $q_{i,t}$: the advanced order \eqref{eq:advanced-order} and the null order, combined through the adoption indicator $a_{i,t}$ as in section~\ref{sec:model-traders}.
\item The market maker absorbs aggregate demand $D_t$ and moves the quote immediately, as in the price update \eqref{eq:price-update}.
\item The exogenous news shock $\sigma\,\varepsilon_{t+1}$ and the autoregressive term $\varphi\, r_t$ realise.
\end{enumerate}
% src: proposal v4 section 3.6, four-step sequence (its references to proposal eqs. (2), (3), (5) map to proposal eq. (2) inline, \eqref{eq:advanced-order}, \eqref{eq:price-update}); CLAUDE.md "Intra-period timing" steps 1 to 4 (identical substance and order)

The sequence separates what the forecast knows from what its users cause. Everything the forecast may legitimately use, returns through period $t$, is in hand before any order is placed; everything its own adopters do to the price, the $\mu D_t$ move of step 3, is in the books before next period's news arrives in step 4. With that separation fixed, it becomes well defined to score the same forecast against two different targets on the same path, the realised return that includes the impact of step 3 or a counterfactual that strips it out, which is the subject of the next subsection. % src: proposal v4 section 3.6 ("Given this timing, the forecast can be evaluated against either of two distinct targets")

\subsection{The realised vs demand-adjusted decomposition}\label{sec:model-decomposition}

The timing of the previous subsection makes it well defined to score the same forecast against two targets on the same path; this subsection constructs them. For evaluation purposes the return law \eqref{eq:return-law} is rewritten as a decomposition into a contemporaneous price-impact component and an ex-feedback remainder. The realised return splits as % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 7; "a decomposition into a contemporaneous price-impact component and an ex-feedback remainder")
\begin{equation}
  r_{t+1} \;=\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; x_{t+1},
  \label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  x_{t+1} \;=\; r_{t+1} \;-\; \mu\, D_t \;=\; \varphi\, r_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}
  \label{eq:demand-adjusted}
\end{equation}
is the demand-adjusted return, the part of the period-$(t+1)$ return that does not depend on contemporaneous adopter trading: the lagged-return component and the exogenous news shock. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 7; "the part of the return that does not depend on contemporaneous adopter trading"; the x = r - mu D form is from the "What x_{t+1} is and is not" paragraph)
The decomposition matters because the rolling AR rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} is, by construction, an estimate of the AR component of the return process and therefore an estimate of $x_{t+1}$ in expectation, while the only return a deployed forecaster can observe and score against is $r_{t+1}$. The report accordingly evaluates the forecast against both targets as primary endpoints with asymmetric interpretations. Realised-return $R^{2}$ scores the forecast against $r_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:decomposition}: the total performance a deployed forecaster actually sees, including whatever part of the return adopter orders coordinated by the public signal have pushed in the forecast direction through $\mu D_t$, and the reading expected to rise with adoption (the self-fulfilling channel). Demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ scores the same forecast against $x_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:demand-adjusted}: the counterfactual that strips out the contemporaneous price impact and asks whether the independent signal the forecast was originally designed to exploit survives once self-fulfilment is removed, and the reading expected to fall (the demand-adjusted-erosion channel). % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 ("an estimate of the AR component of the return process and therefore an estimate of x_{t+1} in expectation"; "realised-return R^2 as the metric a deployed forecaster actually sees and demand-adjusted R^2 as the counterfactual that asks whether the independent signal survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out"; "adopter orders coordinated by the public signal push the realised return in the forecast direction") and section 3.6 (channel names and expected directions)

One definitional caveat does enough work in the results to be worth stating exactly. The demand-adjusted return is \emph{not} the full regression residual of an AR(1) fit on returns. Under the data-generating process \eqref{eq:return-law}, the full residual of a correctly specified regression is the pure exogenous news shock, $\sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - \varphi\, r_t - \mu\, D_t$. The quantity $x_{t+1}$ is constructed by removing the contemporaneous adopter-demand term $\mu D_t$ only, leaving the lagged-return persistence channel $\varphi r_t$ in place. It is best read as a counterfactual: the return path the market would have produced had adopter demand not moved the quote in the current period, so that the predictable AR(1) component still has a chance to show up in the next-period observation. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5, paragraph "What x_{t+1} is and is not" (residual formula sigma eps_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - phi r_t - mu D_t and counterfactual reading, near-verbatim); CLAUDE.md overview ("it is *not* the full regression residual, which would be sigma eps = r_{t+1} - phi r_t - mu D_t")
The distinction is the source of the erosion channel's name. The news shock is independent of everything known at time $t$, so against $\sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$ any forecast carries zero signal in expectation, whatever adoption does; $x_{t+1}$ instead retains exactly the predictable component the forecast was built to recover, so a fall in demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ records the erosion of an independent signal that was genuinely there. That is the claim the name ``demand-adjusted erosion'' makes, and the reading under which the channel is tracked in section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} and beyond. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 ("The counterfactual is what isolates the independent predictive signal that the forecast was originally designed to exploit"); eps_{t+1} iid N(0,1) per proposal eq. (5), reproduced as \eqref{eq:price-update}; channel name per proposal section 3.6 ("Demand-adjusted-erosion channel")


\section{Metrics and experimental design}\label{sec:metrics}

\subsection{Primary forecast-performance endpoints}

\subsubsection{Realised-return $R^{2}$}\label{sec:metrics-r2-realised}

The first primary endpoint scores the shared forecast against the return a deployed forecaster can observe. It is the standard out-of-sample $R^{2}$ of the one-step forecast \eqref{eq:ar-forecast} against the realised return,
\begin{equation}
  R^{2}_{\mathrm{realised}}(t) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(r_{s+1} - \hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}\bigr)^{2}}{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(r_{s+1} - \bar{r}\bigr)^{2}},
  \label{eq:r2-realised}
\end{equation}
where the sums run over the most recent $W$ forecast-target pairs ending at period $t$ and $\bar{r}$ is the mean realised return over that window. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 (R^2_realised display, "computed in a trailing window ending at t"); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2
The evaluation window is a separate knob from the AR estimation window $w$ of section~\ref{sec:model-rules}; every reported run uses $W = 1000$ periods, the notebooks' \texttt{eval\_window} parameter. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['eval_window'] = phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['eval_window'] = 1000

The forecasts entering \eqref{eq:r2-realised} are strictly out of sample, each $\hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}$ being fitted on returns through period $s$ only. The constant benchmark follows one implementation convention worth stating once: \texttt{rolling\_oos\_r2} in \texttt{metrics.py} takes the trailing window's in-sample mean of the target as the benchmark, not a real-time prevailing mean recomputed from the data available before each $s$. The choice slightly favours the benchmark, and with near-zero-mean returns the difference is negligible. % src: src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2 docstring ("The benchmark is the trailing window's in-sample mean of the target, not a real-time prevailing mean, which slightly favours the benchmark. With near-zero-mean returns the difference is negligible. The forecasts themselves are strictly out of sample.")
Because the target $r_{s+1}$ contains the contemporaneous impact term $\mu D_s$ of the decomposition \eqref{eq:decomposition}, this is the total-performance reading of section~\ref{sec:model-decomposition}, expected to rise with adoption through the self-fulfilling channel. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 ("expected to rise with adoption rather than fall, because coordinated adopter trading pushes r_{t+1} in the forecast direction")

\subsubsection{Demand-adjusted-return $R^{2}$}

The second primary endpoint scores the same forecast, on the same path and over the same trailing window, against the demand-adjusted return $x_{s+1}$ of \eqref{eq:demand-adjusted}:
\begin{equation}
  R^{2}_{\mathrm{da}}(t) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(x_{s+1} - \hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}\bigr)^{2}}{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(x_{s+1} - \bar{x}\bigr)^{2}},
  \label{eq:r2-da}
\end{equation}
with $\bar{x}$ the window mean of the demand-adjusted return; the benchmark convention of section~\ref{sec:metrics-r2-realised} applies unchanged. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.2 (R^2_da display with x_{s+1} = r_{s+1} - mu D_s); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2 applied to the demand-adjusted target, e.g. notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb metrics cell (rolling_oos_r2(demand_adjusted, forecasts, eval_window))
The same forecast can earn two different $R^{2}$ readings on the same path because the targets differ by exactly that impact term: adopter orders are aligned with the forecast, so $\mu D_s$ systematically moves the realised target toward the forecast, while a rolling fit that has absorbed the resulting amplified autocorrelation over-predicts the demand-adjusted target, from which the term is removed. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 ("coordinated adopter trading pushes r_{t+1} in the forecast direction") and section 4.1.2 ("the rolling fit picks up an amplified empirical autocorrelation from realised returns and over-predicts the demand-adjusted process")
The two endpoints are reported on equal footing, neither being the canonical evaluation target: $R^{2}_{\mathrm{realised}}$ measures what a deployed forecaster sees, and $R^{2}_{\mathrm{da}}$ measures whether the independent signal the forecast was built to exploit survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out, the demand-adjusted-erosion channel whose thresholds section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} defines. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.2 ("The two R^2 readings are reported on equal footing; neither is the canonical evaluation target, and each answers a different question") and section 4 intro (realised reading is the total performance a deployed forecaster sees; demand-adjusted reading is the counterfactual that tests whether the independent signal survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out)

\subsection{Primary economic endpoint}

The primary economic endpoint of this study is net trading value: whether the forecast still pays once trading is costly. The transaction-cost extension charges a proportional cost $c$ per unit traded, turning the gross profit $\Pi_{i,t+1} = q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$ of section~\ref{sec:model-traders} into a net figure read against the null rule rather than against zero: per period, % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (net trading value, "net profit after transaction costs, reported both in absolute terms and relative to the null benchmark"; null-relative is "reported as the primary economic measure"); section 3.7 transaction-costs bullet ("Subtract proportional trading costs from profits to evaluate whether modest statistical predictability survives economically"); section 3.1.6 (profit eq. 8; "Transaction costs are omitted in the baseline and added as an extension in section 3.7")
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\Pi_{t+1} \;=\; \bigl(q^{(A)}_{t}\, r_{t+1} \;-\; c\,\lvert q^{(A)}_{t}\rvert\bigr) \;-\; \bigl(q^{(0)}_{t}\, r_{t+1} \;-\; c\, E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert\bigr),
  \label{eq:null-relative-profit}
\end{equation}
the adopter's net profit minus the null's, the latter a representative null trader's realised gross profit less its expected cost bill, with $q^{(A)}_{t}$ the common adopter order of \eqref{eq:advanced-order} and $E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert = \sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_{q}$ the null rule's mean absolute position. The rule pays where the mean of \eqref{eq:null-relative-profit} is positive, beating an active but skill-free benchmark net of both rules' costs. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (adopter net profit q^A r - c|q^A| minus a null benchmark accounting for "the null trader's realised gross P&L and its own per-period cost c E[|q^(0)|]"); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell (net_null[t] = null_profit[t] - c * sqrt(2/pi) * sigma_q) and run cell ("q_adv is the common per-period adopter order exported by the simulator"); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.7979 = sqrt(2/pi) * sigma_q at sigma_q = 1.0

Two properties of the benchmark shape this reading in section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}. First, the null's gross profit has positive mean $\mu\sigma_{q}^{2}/N$, $2.5\times10^{-4}$ at the phase 7 parameters: a null trader's own order moves the within-period quote, booking own-impact paper profit. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell comment ("E[null_profit] = mu sigma_q^2 / N (= 2.5e-4 at these parameters), own-impact paper profit"); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['mu'] = 0.05, ['N'] = 200, sigma_q = 1.0 (notebook parameters cell): 0.05 * 1.0 / 200 = 2.5e-4; src/reflexive_market/market.py aggregate_demand (D_t = (1/N) sum_i q_{i,t})
Second, both legs of the comparison scale with the null's position, whose mean absolute size, about $0.80$, is roughly 16 times the adopter cap $\bar{q} = 0.05$. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.798, ['q_cap'] = 0.05, ratio = 15.96; notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell ("a position with E|q^(0)| ~ 0.8, roughly 16x the adopter cap")
The design nevertheless treats the null-relative view as primary, since absolute profit does not control for the cost a no-skill rule itself pays whenever $c > 0$; phase 7 reports it accordingly, with the caveat that position-size asymmetry, not forecast content, dominates the comparison in both cost regimes. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (absolute net profit secondary, "does not control for the cost a null rule itself pays in any cost regime c > 0"); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb done-when cell (null-relative endpoint "dominated by position-size asymmetry in both directions": below the benchmark at c = 0, above it at every c > 0); CLAUDE.md overview ("phase 7 reports it with that caveat")

\subsection{Effective AR coefficient as a market-dynamics diagnostic}\label{sec:metrics-phi}

The two $R^{2}$ endpoints score the forecast; the rolling effective AR coefficient $\hat{\varphi}^{\mathrm{eff}}(t)$ instead tracks the dynamics of the market the forecast trades in. It is estimated from realised returns alone, with no reference to the forecast: \texttt{rolling\_phi} in \texttt{metrics.py} computes, at each period, the lag-1 sample autocorrelation of the most recent $W$ realised returns, with the same trailing window $W = 1000$ as the $R^{2}$ endpoints in every reported run. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("estimated directly from the realised return series in a trailing window"); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_phi (np.corrcoef(seg[:-1], seg[1:]) over the most recent `window` returns ending at each index; first window-1 entries NaN); window = 1000 everywhere: phase_02_benchmark.npz['rolling_window'] = 1000, phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['eval_window'] = 1000, each passed to rolling_phi in the corresponding notebook
With no adoption it should sit at the input $\varphi$ of the return law \eqref{eq:return-law}; section~\ref{sec:phases12-results} verifies that it does, with no drift. % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['mean_phi_trace'] (flat at the input; total range 0.0156 over the periods with a complete window)
Under adoption it should rise above the input value: adopter demand responds positively to recent returns through the shared forecast, so the impact term $\mu D_t$ reinforces the lagged-return component and measured persistence grows, the mechanism behind the realised-$R^{2}$ climb of section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("adopter buying on a positive AR forecast reinforces the lagged-return component through mu D_t, so phi-eff is expected to rise with adoption")

It is a diagnostic rather than a third endpoint because its role is explanatory: a rolling fit that has absorbed this amplified autocorrelation systematically over-predicts the demand-adjusted target, so the rise of $\hat{\varphi}^{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the mechanical reason the two $R^{2}$ readings diverge. % src: proposal v4 section 4 intro ("The remaining quantities are diagnostics, including the effective AR coefficient as a market-dynamics diagnostic that explains why the two R^2 readings move in opposite directions") and section 4.1.3 ("a rolling fit that has absorbed the amplified empirical autocorrelation will systematically over-predict against x_{t+1}")
Sections~\ref{sec:phase4-results} and~\ref{sec:phase5-results} track it against the adoption share, and the relative threshold $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$ of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} summarises its rise across the parameter grid of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5; report line "A*_{R2,da,rel} and A*_{phi,rel}, defined in section sec:metrics-a-star" (phased-construction list, phase 6 item)

\section{Implementation}

\subsection{Software architecture}\label{sec:software-architecture}

The simulator is a small pure-numpy Python package, \texttt{reflexive\_market}, under \texttt{src/}: six working modules plus a seven-line \texttt{\_\_init\_\_.py} holding only a docstring and the version string, 588 lines in total. % src: wc -l src/reflexive_market/*.py (7+47+75+30+150+249+30 = 588)
Four design constraints, fixed in the project working agreement before any simulator code was written, shaped the package. % src: CLAUDE.md, Code conventions section (lines 203-209); scaffold commit 52b885a adds CLAUDE.md with "No simulator or notebook code yet"
First, the mathematics uses numpy alone: AR estimation goes through \texttt{np.linalg.lstsq}, and no pandas, scipy, statsmodels, or scikit-learn import appears anywhere in \texttt{src/}. Second, every random draw flows through a \texttt{numpy.random.Generator} passed in as an explicit argument, and no module touches the global \texttt{np.random} state, so output is fully determined by the seed. Third, package functions never print or plot; parameters, figures, and the saved \texttt{npz} summaries live in the phase notebooks, which import the package. Fourth, each module implements one small, named slice of the model, so that every equation of section~\ref{sec:model} can be traced to code; Table~\ref{tab:modules} gives the mapping. % src: CLAUDE.md module table (lines 54-61); module docstrings in src/reflexive_market/

\texttt{simulate.py} is deliberately the largest module. Its single entry point \texttt{run()} is the only place where state evolves, and it follows the intra-period timing fixed in section~\ref{sec:model-timing}: agents form forecasts on returns up to and including period $t$, adopters and non-adopters submit orders, the market maker absorbs aggregate demand and moves the quote, and only then do the autoregressive term and the exogenous news shock realise. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py module docstring
Its defaults reproduce the phase 1 baseline with null traders only, so later phases extended the same function through keyword arguments (forecast window, risk scale and position cap, adoption parameters) rather than forking the loop. The two adoption mechanisms are mutually exclusive one-step pure functions invoked inside the loop. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py docstring of run(); switching_window "Cannot be combined with non-zero adoption_pi"
The functions in \texttt{metrics.py} score completed runs after the fact; the target-specific critical adoption shares are computed in the phase notebooks rather than in \texttt{metrics.py} because they need the full simulator output, not a return series alone. % src: src/reflexive_market/metrics.py module docstring

\subsection{Phased construction}

The simulator was built in seven strictly ordered phases, a staging discipline fixed in the project working agreement before any simulator code was written; phase $N$ does not start until phase $N-1$ runs end to end, meets its recorded done-when condition, and is committed. % src: proposal v4 section 5 staging table (verify-before-extend ordering); CLAUDE.md "Implementation phases" preamble (lines 65-67, run-end-to-end-and-commit gate); per-phase "Done when" blocks and "How to work on a phase" steps 3 and 6 (lines 256 and 259, done-when confirmation and next-phase gate)
The done-when criteria for phases 1 to 3 are unchanged since the scaffold; those for phases 4 to 7 were restated under the dual-channel framing after all seven phases had been implemented, once that framing had settled as the organising description of the headline result. % src: git diff 52b885a..HEAD -- CLAUDE.md (phase 1-3 "Done when" blocks unchanged; phase 4-7 blocks edited in v3-alignment commit 82ddb73, phase 7's as a dual-channel relabel of "the mechanism"; phase 4-6 blocks relabelled to demand-adjusted terms in c1778ca and phase 6 refined again in ba41aad; all postdating phase 7 commit 1a334cf)
The ordering enforces the design's central feasibility principle: the market must work before the forecasting rule is added, the rule must work before adoption is added, and extensions come only once the baseline produces stable, interpretable results, so that any observed reflexivity effect can be traced to a small number of identifiable ingredients. % src: proposal v4 section 5, "most important feasibility principle" paragraph
Each phase owns one notebook (\texttt{notebooks/phase\_NN\_<name>.ipynb}) with every parameter in a single cell at the top, and saves its figures and \texttt{npz} summaries under \texttt{results/}. All seven phases are implemented; section 6 reports their results in build order. % src: notebooks/README.md phase status table ("All seven phases are implemented"); CLAUDE.md notebook conventions (parameters cell, save cell)

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Baseline market.} Implement the single-asset market with every trader on the random null rule and confirm the empirical lag-1 autocorrelation of returns matches the input $\varphi$ (section~\ref{sec:phases12-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 1 definition
\item \textbf{Benchmark validation.} Rerun the null market across many seeds and verify that the descriptive statistics are stable and the rolling effective AR coefficient shows no drift; this is the no-adoption control behind every later erosion claim (section~\ref{sec:phases12-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 2 definition
\item \textbf{AR forecasting rule, offline.} Add rolling AR fitting and score the forecast against realised returns while no trader acts on it, recovering a small positive out-of-sample $R^{2}$ driven by the $\varphi r_t$ term (section~\ref{sec:phase3-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 3 definition (kept offline, as built; the proposal v4 section 5 table folds forecast-based trading with position caps into its phase 3 row, which the build defers to phase 4)
\item \textbf{Forecast-based trading and stochastic adoption.} Let adopters trade the shared forecast while its use spreads by stochastic diffusion; the dual-channel result first appears here (section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 4 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Performance-based adoption.} Replace diffusion with certainty-equivalent switching and compare the two adoption mechanisms on paired shocks (section~\ref{sec:phase5-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 5 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Experiments and thresholds.} Sweep $(\mu, \varphi, w, p)$ and locate the relative critical adoption shares $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{da},\mathrm{rel}}$ and $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$, defined in section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star}, across the grid (section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 6 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Evaluation and extension.} Summarise the findings under the dual-channel framing and add the transaction-cost extension, reading $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit}}$ against zero and against the null benchmark (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 7 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\end{enumerate}

The phases group into three blocks. Phases 1 to 3 establish the pre-adoption control: a market with no forecast trading and a forecast with no market feedback, each verified on its own. Phases 4 and 5 switch the feedback on and establish the dual-channel mechanism under two different adoption processes. Phases 6 and 7 quantify the mechanism, first across the parameter grid and then in economic terms, with phase 7 also serving as the evaluation summary of the whole build.

\subsection{Baseline market and benchmark validation (phases 1 and 2)}\label{sec:phases12-results}

Phase 1 checks that the simulator reproduces the baseline market of section~\ref{sec:model-market} when every trader follows the random null rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules}. A single run uses $N=200$ traders, $T=5000$ periods, price-impact coefficient $\mu=0.05$, input autoregressive coefficient $\varphi=0.10$ in the return law $r_{t+1}=\varphi r_t+\mu D_t+\sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$, news scale $\sigma=0.01$, null-order scale $\sigma_q=1.0$, and seed 42. % src: notebooks/phase_01_baseline_market.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_01_baseline.npz['seed']
Null orders are independent Gaussian draws, so aggregate demand $D_t$ is directionally uninformative and feeds the return only as unpredictable price-impact noise; the only structure in returns that a forecast could exploit is the $\varphi r_t$ term. The run behaves accordingly. The log price wanders without systematic drift (the mean one-step return of $2.0\times10^{-4}$ sits within 1.4 standard errors of zero), and the return series is roughly stationary with standard deviation $0.0105$. % src: phase_01_baseline.npz['returns'] (mean = 1.998e-4, std = 0.01050, se = std/sqrt(5000) = 1.485e-4); results/figures/phase_01_price_path.png
The empirical lag-1 autocorrelation of returns is $0.103$, matching the input $\varphi=0.10$ within the sampling error of roughly $1/\sqrt{T}\approx0.014$ for a series of this length. % src: phase_01_baseline.npz['phi_empirical'] vs ['phi_input']

Phase 2 reruns the null-rule market across 100 independent seeds (0 through 99) with $N=128$, $T=5000$, and the same market parameters, to confirm the baseline is a stable control rather than a one-seed accident. % src: notebooks/phase_02_benchmark_validation.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_02_benchmark.npz['seeds']
The per-seed descriptive statistics cluster tightly around their theoretical values. The per-seed mean return averages $1.5\times10^{-5}$ with a cross-seed standard deviation of $1.6\times10^{-4}$, consistent with a zero unconditional mean; % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['mean_per_seed'] (mean, std)
the per-seed return standard deviation averages $0.0110$ with cross-seed spread $0.00012$; % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['std_per_seed'] (mean, std)
the per-seed lag-1 autocorrelation averages $0.103$ with spread $0.012$, clustered around the input $\varphi=0.10$; % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['rho_per_seed'] (mean, std) vs ['phi_input']
and the per-seed excess kurtosis averages $-0.0065$ with spread $0.071$, indistinguishable from the Gaussian value of zero. % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['kurt_per_seed'] (mean, std); excess (Fisher) kurtosis per src/reflexive_market/metrics.py kurtosis()
The kurtosis reading, together with the symmetric single-seed return histogram from phase 1, supports treating null-rule returns as approximately Gaussian, as expected when the news shock and the individual null orders are both Gaussian and aggregate demand averages across many traders. % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['kurt_per_seed']; results/figures/phase_01_return_histogram.png
The sharpest control statistic is the rolling effective AR coefficient shown in Figure~\ref{fig:phase02-rolling-phi}. Estimated on a 1000-period sliding window and averaged across seeds, the trace sits near the input value and stays flat: its total range over the 4001 periods with a complete window is $0.016$, with no trend in either direction. % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['mean_phi_trace'] (nanmax - nanmin = 0.0156); ['rolling_window'] = 1000

Later phases attribute movements in forecast $R^{2}$ and in the effective AR coefficient to adoption of the shared forecast; that attribution is meaningful only because the same market with no adoption, simulated over the same horizon, shows no comparable drift.

\subsection{AR forecast performance without adoption (phase 3)}\label{sec:phase3-results}

Phase 3 attaches the rolling AR(1) forecasting rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} to the null-market design of the previous subsection, in offline mode: the forecast is estimated and scored, but no trader acts on it. A single run uses $N=200$ traders, $T=8000$ periods, $\mu=0.05$, $\sigma=0.01$, $\sigma_q=1.0$, and seed 43, with the input autoregressive coefficient raised to $\varphi=0.25$ so the rule has a clearly detectable signal to recover. % src: notebooks/phase_03_ar_forecast.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['phi_input'], ['seed']
Each period the rule fits an AR(1) by least squares on the most recent $w \in \{50, 100, 250\}$ returns and issues a strictly out-of-sample one-step forecast $\hat{r}^{(A)}_{t+1}$, scored post hoc against realised returns by the out-of-sample $R^{2}$ \eqref{eq:r2-realised} against a constant-mean benchmark, tracked on a trailing 1000-period window, and by full-sample mean squared forecast error (MSFE). % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['ar_windows'], ['ar_order'], ['eval_window']; src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2(), msfe()
With zero adoption the realised return and the demand-adjusted return $x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - \mu D_t$ differ only by unpredictable null-order impact, so this subsection reports realised-return performance only; the two targets separate once adopters start trading in phase 4. % src: notebooks/phase_03_ar_forecast.ipynb title cell (cell 0), note on realised vs demand-adjusted return

The rule recovers the signal it was built for. The mean rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ is small, positive for every window, and rising with window length: $0.013$ for $w=50$, $0.033$ for $w=100$, and $0.047$ for $w=250$. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['summary'][:, 2]
Figure~\ref{fig:phase03-rolling-oos-r2} shows the time profile: the traces co-move because they score one path, longer windows sit almost everywhere higher, and the share of periods with positive $R^{2}$ rises with $w$. The $w=250$ trace is positive at every evaluated period (minimum $0.020$), $w=100$ in $98.5$ percent of periods, while $w=50$ dips below zero in roughly $16$ percent. % src: rolling_oos_r2 traces recomputed from notebooks/phase_03_ar_forecast.ipynb parameters cell and seed 43 (reproduces npz summary exactly); w=50 sits below both longer windows at every common period, but w=100 exceeds w=250 in 68 of 6751 common periods (6781 to 6915, max gap 0.0047), hence "almost everywhere"; results/figures/phase_03_rolling_oos_r2.png
These levels are consistent with the return law's ceiling: an oracle knowing $\varphi$ exactly would attain a population $R^{2}$ of $\varphi^{2}=0.0625$, which the longer windows approach from below as estimation noise shrinks. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['phi_input'] = 0.25; ceiling = phi^2 follows from the return law (eq. 6 of the proposal) with iid null-order demand
The coefficient estimates tell the same story: the rolling $\hat{\varphi}$ fluctuates around the input value, with means $0.195$, $0.217$, and $0.229$ and standard deviations $0.145$, $0.103$, and $0.063$ for $w=50$, $100$, and $250$; the mild downward gap reflects the small-sample bias of least-squares AR(1) estimation together with single-path sampling noise, and closes as the window lengthens. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['summary'][:, 3] (means); stds recomputed from the notebook parameters cell and seed 43; results/figures/phase_03_ar_coefficients.png
Full-sample MSFE is nearly flat across windows ($1.18\times10^{-4}$ to $1.15\times10^{-4}$) and the rolling MSFE traces show no trend, as expected when no trader acts on the forecast. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['summary'][:, 1]; results/figures/phase_03_rolling_msfe.png
Sign accuracy, a diagnostic rather than an endpoint, is $0.56$ for the two shorter windows and $0.57$ for $w=250$. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['summary'][:, 4] (0.5592, 0.5592, 0.5732)

This completes the pre-adoption baseline. The previous subsection showed that the market does not drift when no one trades on a forecast; this one shows that the rolling rule recovers the linear-persistence signal at a known level, a small, stable, positive out-of-sample $R^{2}$ due entirely to the $\varphi r_t$ term.

\subsection{Dual-channel result under stochastic adoption (phase 4)}\label{sec:phase4-results}

Phase 4 turns the offline forecast of the previous subsection into a tradeable rule and lets its use spread. Adopters map the shared rolling AR(1) forecast ($w=250$) into positions through the mean-variance demand \eqref{eq:bh-demand}, clipped at the position cap as in \eqref{eq:advanced-order}; non-adopters keep the random null rule. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['forecast_window']
With combined risk scaling $a\sigma^{2}=0.001$ and per-trader cap $\bar{q}=0.05$ the cap binds in nearly every period, so adopters are effectively fixed-size traders on the forecast's sign and their impact stays comparable in magnitude to the $\varphi r_t$ term rather than dominating it. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['risk_scale'], ['q_cap']; notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (comment on q_cap)
Adoption follows the stochastic diffusion of \eqref{eq:diffusion-entry} and \eqref{eq:diffusion-exit} in three regimes: zero adoption ($\pi=0$), slow diffusion ($\pi=3\times10^{-4}$), and fast diffusion ($\pi=10^{-3}$), each with exit probability $\delta=0$, run on paired shocks from the shared seed 71 so that differences across regimes are attributable to adoption alone. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['regime_names'], ['regime_pi'], ['regime_delta'], ['seed']
The market is otherwise the phase 3 configuration ($N=200$, $T=8000$, $\mu=0.05$, input $\varphi=0.25$, $\sigma=0.01$, $\sigma_q=1.0$), with every rolling metric computed on a trailing 1000-period window. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['N'], ['T'], ['mu'], ['phi_input'], ['eval_window']; notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (sigma_news, sigma_q)
Adoption is held at zero until period 1250, the first period with a complete metric window, and the headline comparison contrasts a low-adoption window (periods 1250 to 2750) with a high-adoption window (the final 1500 periods), by which point the slow regime has reached a mean adoption share of 0.836 and the fast regime 0.994. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cells 1 and 4 (adoption_start_t = 1250, summary_span = 1500); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][:, 1]

Against realised returns, the forecast improves as adoption rises. In the high-adoption window the fast regime's rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ against $r_{t+1}$ is 0.190, versus 0.070 for the zero-adoption control on the same shocks (0.065 in the control's own late window), with the slow regime in between at 0.165. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 3] = 0.1904, [0, 2] = 0.0695, [0, 3] = 0.0653, [1, 3] = 0.1651
The control row is the right low-adoption anchor: diffusion at $\pi=10^{-3}$ is fast enough that the fast regime's own early window already averages an adoption share of 0.505, with realised $R^{2}$ already lifted to 0.094. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 0] = 0.5048, [2, 2] = 0.0942
The mechanism is the decomposition $r_{t+1}=\mu D_t + x_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:decomposition}: every adopter trades in the direction of the same public forecast, so coordinated adopter demand pushes the realised return in the forecast direction, and the forecast scores partly against its own price impact. The left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-oos-r2-vs-adoption} shows that this is not a one-seed accident: pooled across 100 Monte Carlo seeds, the mean realised-return $R^{2}$ rises monotonically in adoption share. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cells 21 and 23 (num_seeds_mc = 100, mc_base_seed = 1000); results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png

The same forecast on the same path is simultaneously losing the signal it was built to exploit. Scored against the demand-adjusted return $x_{t+1}=r_{t+1}-\mu D_t$, the counterfactual that strips out the contemporaneous demand impact while leaving the lagged-return component $\varphi r_t$ and the news shock in place (it is not the full regression residual; section~\ref{sec:model-decomposition}), the rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ falls to 0.039 in the fast regime's high-adoption window, versus 0.082 under the control, with the slow regime at 0.047. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 5] = 0.0386, [0, 4] = 0.0821, [1, 5] = 0.0471; definition per proposal sections 3.1.5 and 3.6 (eq. 7)
This reading asks whether the forecast still carries independent predictive content once self-fulfilment is removed, and the answer weakens as adoption rises: in the right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-oos-r2-vs-adoption} the Monte Carlo mean declines as adoption rises, with the drop steepening at high adoption shares. % src: results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png

The rolling effective AR coefficient is the mechanical link between the two readings. Estimated as the lag-1 autocorrelation of realised returns on the same trailing window, it rises from 0.282 under the control, close to the input $\varphi=0.25$, to 0.447 in the fast regime's high-adoption window (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-phi-vs-adoption}). % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470, ['phi_input'] = 0.25
Adopter demand responds positively to recent returns through the forecast, so the impact term $\mu D_t$ reinforces the lagged-return component and measured persistence grows with adoption. The rolling AR fit estimates its coefficient from realised returns, absorbs this amplified effective coefficient, and therefore systematically over-predicts the demand-adjusted process, whose own AR coefficient stays at the input 0.25 by construction; that over-prediction is the demand-adjusted decline of the previous paragraph. % src: mechanism per proposal section 4.1.3 (effective AR coefficient diagnostic); notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 16 (markdown)
The control, meanwhile, moves far less across the same two windows (realised 0.070 to 0.065, demand-adjusted 0.082 to 0.068, effective coefficient 0.282 to 0.278), so the movements in the diffusion regimes are adoption effects, not time effects. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, :] = (0, 0, 0.0695, 0.0653, 0.0821, 0.0676, 0.2821, 0.2776)

This is the dual-channel result, and it is the headline finding of the report: adoption raises realised-return predictive performance through the self-fulfilment channel while eroding the independent demand-adjusted signal, on the same path and from the same forecast. Which reading a forecaster sees depends entirely on whether the evaluation target includes the forecaster's own price impact.

\subsection{Endogenous CE-based switching (phase 5)}\label{sec:phase5-results}

Phase 5 makes adoption respond to how the forecast is actually paying: the stochastic diffusion of \eqref{eq:diffusion-entry} and \eqref{eq:diffusion-exit} is replaced by the risk-adjusted performance-based switching of section~\ref{sec:model-adoption}. Each period the simulator records the realised one-period profit $q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$ for a representative trader under each rule: the common capped adopter position, and a representative non-adopter's order. % src: notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 0; src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() docstring (null_profit, advanced_profit, eq. 8)
Each rule's certainty equivalent $\mathrm{CE}^{(k)}_t=\bar{\Pi}^{(k)}_t-\tfrac{a}{2}\,\hat{\sigma}^{2,(k)}_t$, the rolling mean profit net of a risk penalty on its rolling variance, is computed over $W_A=500$ periods with $a=1$, per \eqref{eq:ce}, and each non-adopter switches to the advanced rule with the logistic probability $\Lambda(\alpha+\beta S_t)$ of \eqref{eq:logit-switch} in the score gap $S_t=\mathrm{CE}^{(A)}_t-\mathrm{CE}^{(0)}_t$, with $\alpha=-5$ and $\beta=10000$. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_window'] = 500, ['switching_a'] = 1.0, ['switching_alpha'] = -5.0, ['switching_beta'] = 10000.0; proposal section 3.5, eqs. (14) and (15)
At a zero score the implied switch probability is $\Lambda(-5)\approx0.0067$ per period, and score movements of order $10^{-4}$ move it strongly in either direction. % src: computed from phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_alpha'] and ['switching_beta']: 1/(1+e^5) = 0.00669; beta = 1e4 makes a score of 1e-4 worth one logit
Everything else stays at the phase 4 configuration with adoption starting at period 1250; the comparator is the fast stochastic regime ($\pi=10^{-3}$) rerun on paired shocks from the shared seed 71, so any difference is attributable to the adoption mechanism. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['stochastic_pi'] = 1e-3, ['seed'] = 71, ['T'], ['N'], ['mu'], ['phi_input'], ['eval_window']; notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (adoption_start_t = 1250)

The adoption path changes shape; the erosion does not. The endogenous share is steplike. Through most of the run-up the score hovers slightly below zero (negative in 88 percent of pre-saturation periods, held down in part by the null rule's positive own-impact profit mean $\mu\sigma_q^{2}/N=2.5\times10^{-4}$), and at the median pre-saturation score the implied switch rate is 0.00049 per period, about half the comparator's diffusion rate, so the share mostly creeps; episodes of positive score raise the per-period inflow by a factor of about 3.6, producing the bursts. % src: recomputed from notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb parameters cell with seed 71 (deterministic rerun reproduces npz['summary'] exactly); all stats over the pre-saturation window t in [1250, 2842): S_t < 0 in 1397 of 1592 periods; median S = -2.61e-4, Lambda(-5 + 1e4 * (-2.61e-4)) = 0.00049, vs stochastic_pi = 1e-3; mean per-period share gain 0.00172 (S > 0) vs 0.00048 (S <= 0), consistent with the implied rate; E[null profit] = mu*sigma_q^2/N = 0.05*1.0/200 = 2.5e-4 per src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() docstring
The endogenous run reaches full adoption by period 2842, while the stochastic share crosses 0.99 only at period 6164. % src: same rerun (first A = 1 at t = 2842; stochastic first A >= 0.99 at t = 6164); results/figures/phase_05_adoption_share.png
Yet over the same low- and high-adoption windows as the previous subsection the two regimes give nearly identical erosion readings: the demand-adjusted out-of-sample $R^{2}$ falls from 0.071 to 0.039 under stochastic diffusion and from 0.076 to 0.037 under performance switching, while the rolling effective AR coefficient rises from 0.320 to 0.447 and from 0.315 to 0.449 respectively, at mean adoption shares of 0.505 to 0.994 and 0.455 to 1.000. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['summary'] (rows: stochastic diffusion, performance switching; cols: A_low, A_high, R2da_low, R2da_high, phi_low, phi_high)
Figure~\ref{fig:phase05-erosion-vs-adoption} traces both diagnostics against the adoption share itself: the two regimes start from the same pre-adoption values, decline and rise along overlapping paths, and converge at full adoption. % src: results/figures/phase_05_erosion_vs_adoption.png; rerun: first finite rolling demand-adjusted R^2 = 0.1041 at t = 1249 (identical across regimes pre-adoption)
The performance-switching curve sits somewhat above the stochastic one on the demand-adjusted panel at intermediate shares; this is a lag artefact: the endogenous run sweeps those shares too quickly for the trailing 1000-period metric window to refresh (235 periods between shares 0.6 and 0.95, against 1945 for the diffusion run). % src: rerun: 235 periods with A in [0.6, 0.95] (performance switching) vs 1945 (stochastic diffusion); results/figures/phase_05_erosion_vs_adoption.png left panel

Endogenous switching therefore changes when the market visits each adoption level, not what adoption does once there: at matched shares both mechanisms produce the same demand-adjusted decline and the same effective-coefficient amplification. The dual-channel result of the previous subsection is thus not an artefact of the exogenous diffusion assumption; erosion is a property of the adoption share, however the share is generated. The run also shows why the mechanism reinforces rather than brakes adoption in the cost-free baseline: adopter trades strengthen the autoregressive component of realised returns, so the advanced rule's certainty equivalent pulls level and then ahead as adoption builds (the score's pre-saturation maximum arrives one period before full adoption), and nothing pushes the score decisively negative at high adoption. % src: rerun: max pre-saturation S_t = +3.0e-4 at t = 2841, one period before first A = 1 at t = 2842; results/figures/phase_05_switching_score.png (the larger spike to +0.0064 occurs after full adoption)
Whether transaction costs change that is taken up in section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}.

\subsection{Parameter sweep and critical adoption shares (phase 6)}\label{sec:phase6-results}

Phase 6 turns the single-configuration findings of phases 4 and 5 into a map. The sweep crosses $\mu\in\{0.025, 0.05, 0.075, 0.10, 0.15\}$, $\varphi\in\{0.05,\dots,0.30\}$, forecast windows $w\in\{100, 250, 500\}$, and AR orders $p\in\{1, 2, 5, 10\}$, with AR(1) the baseline rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} and the higher orders a robustness extension, at 10 seeds per cell (3600 runs). % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['mu_grid'], ['phi_grid'], ['w_grid'], ['forecast_p_grid'], ['num_seeds']; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 1
Everything else follows the phase 4 design ($N=200$, $T=8000$, trailing 1000-period metrics) under slow stochastic diffusion ($\pi=3\times10^{-4}$, $\delta=0$). Adoption stays at zero until a 1500-period baseline window has completed, so each run measures its own pre-adoption baseline; by the end of the run the share reaches roughly 0.8. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['N'], ['T'], ['eval_window'], ['adoption_pi'], ['baseline_span']; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 4 (adoption_start_t_w = w + eval_window + baseline_span); ['line_curve_adoption_share'] ends at A = 0.78, matching the diffusion expectation 1-(1-pi)^(T-t0) = 0.78 to 0.80 across w
Seeds are paired across $p$: the same $(w,\varphi,\mu,\text{seed})$ gives the same shocks and adoption path at every order, so differences across $p$ isolate the forecast specification. % src: notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 4 (seed formula omits the AR-order index)

The zero-crossing threshold $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{da}}$ of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} fires only where price impact is strong or the baseline signal is near zero; at the phase 4 and 5 settings the demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ falls but stays positive. Phase 6 therefore reports the relative analogue, fixed before the sweep was run and defined wherever the low-adoption baseline is positive. % src: proposal section 4.3 (relative threshold pre-registered and labelled A*_{R2,da,rel}); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_da_grid'][0] (negative tail cells concentrate at high mu and in near-zero-baseline rows); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 5] = 0.039 at mu = 0.05; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cells 0 and 3 (a_star set to NaN when the baseline is not positive)
$A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{da},\mathrm{rel}}$ is the smallest adoption share, past the baseline window, at which the rolling demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ has fallen to half its own low-adoption baseline (threshold factor 0.5); the same convention gives $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{realised},\mathrm{rel}}$ on the realised target and $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$ on the effective AR coefficient, the smallest share at which the rising effective coefficient reaches 1.5 times its baseline. % src: notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 3 (find_a_star, run_seed); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5, ['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5
A cell reports the mean crossing share over its crossing seeds, and only when at least half of the 10 seeds cross, so a noisy minority cannot set it. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['min_hit_rate'] = 0.5; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 5 (hit-rate denominator is all seeds)
One caveat applies throughout: the rolling metrics trail adoption by up to the 1000-period evaluation window, so each $A^{*}$ is a detection point, an upper bound on the erosion onset, not the onset itself. % src: notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 0 (measurement caveat); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['eval_window'] = 1000

Figure~\ref{fig:phase06-a-star-r2} maps the demand-adjusted threshold for the AR(1) rule: it is reached in 67 of the 90 cells, at a mean detected share of 0.29. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'][0] (67 of 90 finite; nanmean = 0.292)
Two regularities organise the map. The crossing arrives later as the input $\varphi$ grows, from very low shares in the $\varphi=0.10$ row to 0.37 to 0.65 at $\varphi=0.30$: the threshold scales with a baseline that grows with $\varphi$, so stronger signals need more adoption to lose half their content. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'][0] (w = 250 row means rise from 0.04 at phi = 0.10 to 0.52 at phi = 0.30; phi = 0.30 values span 0.37 to 0.65 across w); ['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (phi-row means -0.013 to 0.106)
And the crossing is roughly flat in $\mu$ (column means 0.25 to 0.31, no monotone trend), because the baseline $R^{2}$ also grows with $\mu$ and the relative threshold scales with it. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'][0] (nan-aware column means by mu = 0.251, 0.285, 0.311, 0.310, 0.297); ['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (column means rise from 0.027 to 0.052 in mu)
The $\mu$ gradient lives instead in the depth of the erosion: the mean demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ over the final 1500 periods falls with $\mu$ in every $\varphi$ row, from $+0.08$ at $(\varphi=0.30,\mu=0.025)$ to $-0.28$ at $(\varphi=0.05,\mu=0.15)$ for $w=250$, so at the strongest price impact the independent signal is not merely halved but pushed below zero, the absolute erosion criterion of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star}. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_da_grid'][0] (w = 250: 0.078 and -0.281 at the cited cells; mu-column means over the AR(1) grid fall 0.021, 0.007, -0.015, -0.048, -0.152; rows are monotone in mu except a 3e-4 uptick at w = 250, phi = 0.30, flat at display precision)
The effective-coefficient threshold (Figure~\ref{fig:phase06-a-star-phi}) is reached in 82 of 90 cells at a mean share of 0.38, and there the $\mu$ gradient shows in the threshold itself: at moderate-to-high $\varphi$, larger $\mu$ pulls $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$ earlier ($w=250$, $\varphi=0.25$: 0.66 at $\mu=0.05$, 0.33 at $\mu=0.15$). The eight unreached cells sit at low $\mu$ and $\varphi\geq0.20$, where feedback is too weak to amplify an already strong coefficient by half. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_phi'][0] (82 of 90 finite, nanmean = 0.379; [0, 1, 4, :] = nan, 0.656, 0.558, 0.524, 0.333; NaN cells at mu = 0.025 except one at mu = 0.05, all phi >= 0.20)

The realised-return threshold is the designed non-result. $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{realised},\mathrm{rel}}$ is reached in only 28 of the 90 AR(1) cells, always early (mean share 0.06, maximum 0.21), with 26 of the 28 hits at $\varphi\leq0.20$ and a median baseline realised $R^{2}$ of 0.014 at the hit cells. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (28 finite; nanmean = 0.062; max = 0.208); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] at hit cells (median 0.014, 86 percent below 0.02; 26 of 28 hits at phi <= 0.20)

The substantive realised-channel finding is the absence of crossings elsewhere: the tail-window realised $R^{2}$ rises with both $\varphi$ and $\mu$, up to 0.32 at the strongest cell, the self-fulfilment channel of phase 4 at grid scale. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (max 0.318 at w = 500, phi = 0.30, mu = 0.15; w = 250 slice spans 0.002 to 0.313, rising in phi and mu); proposal section 4.3 (A*_{R2,realised} typically not reached, the absence is a finding)
The same caveat covers the bottom rows of Figure~\ref{fig:phase06-a-star-r2}: at $\varphi\leq0.10$ the demand-adjusted baseline is also near zero, and those rows fail the filter or fire at near-zero shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 and 0.10 row means -0.013 and -0.003); ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (phi = 0.10 crossings at 0.00 to 0.15)

The dual-channel pattern survives every AR order tested. At each $p$ the effective-coefficient threshold is reached on 80 to 82 of 90 cells at mean shares 0.38 to 0.41, the tail demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ deepens with $\mu$, and the realised threshold stays sparse (15 to 28 cells) and confined to low shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_phi'] by p (82, 82, 81, 80 finite; nanmeans 0.379 to 0.407); ['tail_R2_da_grid'] (mu-column means fall in mu at every p); ['a_star_R2_realised'] by p (28, 26, 23, 15 finite; nanmeans 0.062 to 0.084)
Across the full four-order grid the demand-adjusted threshold is reached in 196 of 360 cells (54 percent, per-seed hit rate 0.53) at a mean share of 0.27, against a realised-channel hit rate of 0.26. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'] (196 of 360 finite; nanmean = 0.273); ['hit_rate_R2_da'].mean() = 0.530; ['hit_rate_R2_realised'].mean() = 0.256
Reachability falls with order (67, 59, 42, 28 of 90 cells for $p=1, 2, 5, 10$) because the baseline falls with it (grid means 0.035, 0.028, 0.004, $-0.041$): a higher-order rule fits more coefficients on the same window and starts with less out-of-sample signal, so more cells fail the positive-baseline requirement or the hit-rate filter. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'] by p; ['baseline_R2_da_grid'] by p (grid nanmeans)

The erosion of the independent signal is therefore a property of the mechanism, not of the AR(1) specification.

\subsection{Transaction-cost extension and economic endpoint (phase 7)}\label{sec:phase7-results}

Phase 7 closes the results with the transaction-cost extension and the economic endpoint it enables. A proportional cost $c$ is charged on every unit traded, so per-period adopter net profit is $q^{(A)}_t r_{t+1} - c\,\lvert q^{(A)}_t\rvert$; the dynamics are unchanged and costs enter only the profit accounting, so 50 paired-shock seed runs of the phase 4 slow-diffusion design support the whole sweep over $c\in\{0, 0.001, 0.002, 0.003, 0.004, 0.005, 0.0075, 0.01\}$. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb parameters cell (costs enter only the P&L accounting); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['c_grid'], ['num_seeds'] = 50, ['adoption_pi'] = 3e-4
Net profit is pooled across seeds and time into 30 adoption-share bins; each cost level reports the $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit}}$ of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} in both conventions: $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,abs}}$, the smallest share at which mean absolute net profit crosses zero, and $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,rel}}$, the smallest share at which mean net profit relative to the null benchmark crosses zero. % src: proposal section 4.3 (A*_profit reported separately for the null-relative primary and absolute secondary conventions); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb first_zero_crossing cell
The benchmark, the null leg of the primary economic endpoint \eqref{eq:null-relative-profit}, is the representative null trader's realised gross profit minus its own cost bill $c\,E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert$, with $E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert=\sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_q\approx0.80$. % src: proposal section 4.1.4 (net profit relative to the null benchmark, primary); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.7979

At zero cost the rule pays at every observed share and pays more as adoption grows, from a pooled mean of $1.2\times10^{-4}$ per period at $A<0.2$ to $2.0\times10^{-4}$ at $A>0.6$. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'][0, 1] = 1.19e-4, [0, 2] = 2.02e-4
That growth, roughly $0.8\times10^{-4}$ between the two windows, recurs at every cost level: cost shifts the absolute curves down in parallel and leaves the slope in adoption untouched (Figure~\ref{fig:phase07-net-profit}, left). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 minus column 1 = 8.3e-5 in every row)
The absolute threshold therefore exists only in a narrow band: the binned curve is positive at every observed share for $c\leq0.002$, negative everywhere for $c\geq0.005$, and crosses zero only at $c=0.003$ and $0.004$, at $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,abs}}=0.35$ and $0.72$ (Figure~\ref{fig:phase07-a-star-profit}). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['a_star_profit_abs'] = (nan, nan, nan, 0.35, 0.717, nan, nan, nan); ['binned_abs_means'] (all 28 populated bins positive at c <= 0.002, all negative at c >= 0.005)
The crossing runs from below, negative at low adoption and positive at high: a profitability onset, the share self-fulfilment needs before the rule covers its costs, the reverse of the from-above erosion reading the threshold was designed to detect. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'][3, 1:3] = (-3.0e-5, +5.3e-5), [4, 1:3] = (-8.0e-5, +0.3e-5); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb direction note above the first_zero_crossing cell
The null-relative curve produces no meaningful crossing at all. At $c=0$ it is slightly negative in 26 of the 28 populated bins and at both pooled ends ($-2.0\times10^{-4}$ at $A<0.2$, $-0.5\times10^{-4}$ at $A>0.6$); at every positive cost level it is uniformly positive. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['binned_rel_means'][0] (26 of 28 bins with counts > 10 are negative), ['summary'][0, 3] = -1.96e-4, [0, 4] = -0.53e-4; ['binned_rel_means'][1:] (every populated bin positive at every c >= 0.001)
The single detected crossing, $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,rel}}\approx0.68$ at $c=0$, is one bin popping to $+1.7\times10^{-4}$ against an in-bin standard deviation of $0.011$: noise around a systematically negative mean, not a threshold. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['a_star_profit_rel'][0] = 0.683; ['binned_rel_means'][0] (bin 0.683 = +1.71e-4, bin 0.717 = -0.76e-4); ['binned_rel_stds'][0] (0.0109 at that bin)

Both null-relative readings are statements about position size rather than forecast content. A null trader's own order moves the within-period quote, so its expected gross profit is not zero but $\mu\sigma_q^{2}/N=2.5\times10^{-4}$ per period of own-impact paper profit, booked on a mean absolute position $E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert\approx0.80$, roughly 16 times the adopter cap $\bar{q}=0.05$. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell comment (E[null profit] = mu sigma_q^2 / N); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['mu'] = 0.05, ['N'] = 200, sigma_q = 1.0 give 2.5e-4; ['null_mean_abs'] / ['q_cap'] = 15.96
At $c=0$ that term exceeds adopter gross profit at every observed share, so the null-relative mean is systematically, if slightly, negative throughout; only the deficit's narrowing with adoption is real. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'][0] (adopter gross 1.2e-4 to 2.0e-4 against null 2.5e-4; deficit narrows from -2.0e-4 to -0.5e-4); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb done-when cell
At $c>0$ the same asymmetry flips sign through the cost bill: the null pays cost on $\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert\approx0.80$ while the adopter pays on at most $0.05$, so the curve sits uniformly above zero at a level set by the cost difference rather than by the forecast. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (rel columns rise by 7.48e-4 per 0.001 of c, matching c times (E|q0| - q_cap) = 0.748c)
All profits, null and adopter alike, are marked at the post-impact quote with no unwind flow, so every reading here is a paper profit under the model's own accounting. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb closing accounting note ("Connecting back to the proposal")
The null-relative endpoint is therefore implemented exactly as designed but dominated by position-size asymmetry in both directions, and the absolute endpoint is the cleaner economic read in this baseline. On that read adopter mean profit grows with adoption at every cost level tested; transaction costs set only the level at which the growth starts to pay. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 exceeds column 1 in every row); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb done-when cell

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of the dual-channel mechanism}

The dual-channel result of section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} is best read not as two findings that happen to coexist but as one mechanism seen through two evaluation targets. Adoption makes the shared forecast self-fulfilling with respect to the target a deployed forecaster actually observes: adopters trade in the forecast's direction, their coordinated demand moves the price the same way, and the realised return being scored becomes partly a product of the forecast itself (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-oos-r2-vs-adoption}, left panel). % src: restates sec:phase4-results, mechanism per the decomposition \eqref{eq:decomposition}; results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png (left panel is the realised target)
Read against the demand-adjusted target, the counterfactual return of \eqref{eq:decomposition} with the contemporaneous price impact removed, the same adoption is purely degrading: the rolling fit calibrates to the amplified persistence of realised returns (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-phi-vs-adoption}) and systematically over-predicts an underlying process whose persistence has not changed. % src: restates sec:phase4-results (demand-adjusted decline, effective-coefficient mechanism); results/figures/phase_04_phi_vs_adoption_mc.png
Self-fulfilment and erosion are two faces of the same coordinated order flow, which is why they arrive together on the same paths in section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} and persist across the parameter sweep of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}.

For a deployed forecaster the practical lesson is that realised-return $R^{2}$ over-reports the forecasting signal once the forecast has users with price impact. The realised reading conflates two sources of measured performance: the stable statistical signal the rule was trained to recover, the $\varphi r_t$ persistence of \eqref{eq:return-law}, and the price-impact channel created by the rule's own adopters. As adoption grows the two components move in opposite directions, so the headline number improves exactly while the independent content it appears to certify is being eroded; Figure~\ref{fig:phase06-a-star-r2} maps the adoption shares at which that independent content has already lost half of its own low-adoption baseline. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5 (half-baseline convention per sec:metrics-a-star); results/figures/phase_06_a_star_R2_heatmap.png
Evaluation against realised outcomes therefore cannot distinguish a forecast that predicts the market from one the market has come to obey. The demand-adjusted target is the audit that separates the two, and constructing it requires exactly what an external evaluator usually lacks: the period's aggregate order flow $D_t$ and the impact coefficient $\mu$. % src: x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - mu D_t per \eqref{eq:decomposition}; inputs are D_t and mu, observable inside the simulator but not to an outside evaluator

For theory, the result bears on the performative-prediction view of deployed models, in which a prediction's measured accuracy depends on the behaviour the prediction induces, and in which performance is commonly treated as splitting into a stable component and a deployment-driven component. The simulator makes that split an explicit, measurable object in a market with price impact: the demand-adjusted target identifies the stable component, the gap between the realised and demand-adjusted readings measures the deployment-driven component, and the threshold maps of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results} chart how much adoption it takes for that component to move each diagnostic by a fixed fraction of its own baseline. % src: positioning claim only, citations deferred to the Background section; phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5, ['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5 (the fixed fractions)

\subsection{Why the effective AR coefficient rises with adoption}\label{sec:discussion-phi}

The rise in measured persistence documented in section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}, from 0.282 under the zero-adoption control to 0.447 in the fast regime's high-adoption window, is mechanical: it follows from chaining three ingredients of section~\ref{sec:model}. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470
First, at $p=1$ the shared forecast \eqref{eq:ar-forecast} is an affine function of the latest return, with an estimated slope that tracks the fitting window's lag-1 autocorrelation, noisy but centred well above zero throughout these runs, so the forecast typically carries the sign of $r_t$ when the lagged term outweighs the small intercept. % src: src/reflexive_market/forecast.py fit_ar (OLS slope on the lagged design); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][:, 6:8] (rolling lag-1 autocorrelation between 0.278 and 0.447 in every reported window); results/figures/phase_03_ar_coefficients.png (w = 250 slope estimate fluctuates around the input 0.25 with occasional dips toward zero, hence "noisy but centred well above zero" and "typically")
Second, adopters turn the forecast into demand, with one wrinkle: positions are not proportional to its magnitude. The cap in \eqref{eq:advanced-order} binds in nearly every period at the phase 4 settings (section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}), so each adopter trades a fixed size $\bar{q}$ in the forecast's direction, and the adopter block adds $A_t\,\bar{q}$, signed by the forecast and hence typically by $r_t$, to aggregate demand \eqref{eq:aggregate-demand}. Sign-following rather than proportional demand changes the strength of what follows, not its direction. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 ("the clip binds in nearly every period"); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['q_cap'] = 0.05, ['risk_scale'] = 0.001; adopter contribution A_t q^(A) per src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (orders = (1 - adoption) * null + adoption * q_adv, averaged in \eqref{eq:aggregate-demand})
Third, the impact term $\mu D_t$ enters the return law \eqref{eq:return-law} with a positive sign, so the coordinated block pushes $r_{t+1}$ in the direction of $r_t$. % src: \eqref{eq:return-law} (mu > 0 per sec:model-market)

A component of the next return that co-moves with the current one is, by construction, extra lag-1 autocorrelation in realised returns, exactly what the diagnostic of section~\ref{sec:metrics-phi} computes. Its amplitude grows with the adopter mass, of order $\mu A_t \bar{q}$, so the measured coefficient climbs steadily with the adoption share rather than jumping (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-phi-vs-adoption}). % src: results/figures/phase_04_phi_vs_adoption_mc.png (smooth monotone rise across adoption-share bins, roughly 0.25 to 0.42)
A naive reading predicts the opposite, that market-maker absorption of order flow should damp persistence; it fails because $\mu D_t$ adds to the next-period return rather than offsetting it. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("A naive reading ... does not survive once the sign of the price-impact term is tracked carefully")
Being structural, the rise appears under both adoption schemes at matched shares (section~\ref{sec:phase5-results}) and across the parameter grid of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['summary'] (phi 0.320 to 0.447 stochastic, 0.315 to 0.449 switching, as reported in sec:phase5-results); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_phi'] (A*_{phi,rel} populated across the grid)

\subsection{Boundary conditions and the realised-channel non-result}\label{sec:discussion-boundary}

The realised-return channel shows no systematic erosion in any regime tested, an outcome the evaluation design anticipated by giving that channel its own threshold. As section~\ref{sec:phase6-results} reports, $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{realised},\mathrm{rel}}$ is reached in only 28 of the 90 AR(1) cells, against 67 for the demand-adjusted threshold, and the per-seed hit rate over the full four-order grid is 0.26 versus 0.53. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (28 of 90 finite), ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (67 of 90 finite), ['hit_rate_R2_realised'].mean() = 0.256, ['hit_rate_R2_da'].mean() = 0.530
The hits that do occur sit at very low adoption, 23 of the 28 below $A=0.10$, and in rows where the baseline realised $R^{2}$ is small: 26 of the 28 are at $\varphi\leq0.20$, with a median baseline of 0.014 at the hit cells. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (23 of 28 finite values below 0.10; hits by phi row = 1, 8, 10, 7, 2, 0, so 26 of 28 at phi <= 0.20); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] at hit cells (median 0.014)
They are the noise crossings of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}: a near-zero baseline puts the half-baseline threshold at essentially zero, within reach of rolling noise.

The grid also bounds each positive claim. The demand-adjusted erosion, the core finding, holds in 67 of 90 AR(1) cells and 196 of 360 over all four AR orders, and deepens with $\mu$ throughout; sections~\ref{sec:phase5-results} and \ref{sec:phase6-results} show it robust to the adoption mechanism and the AR order across paired-shock seeds. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'][0] (67 of 90 finite) and full array (196 of 360 finite); ['tail_R2_da_grid'][0] (mu-column means fall 0.021, 0.007, -0.015, -0.048, -0.152); robustness per sec:phase5-results and sec:phase6-results
The realised-channel amplification claim belongs to the high-$\mu$, high-$\varphi$ corner where the dual-channel pattern is most visible: at $\varphi\geq0.25$ and $\mu\geq0.10$ the tail-window realised $R^{2}$ reaches 0.19 to 0.32, exceeding its own baseline by 0.15 to 0.24. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi >= 0.25, mu >= 0.10 block spans 0.191 to 0.318) minus ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] (lift spans 0.152 to 0.240 on that block)
Outside that corner the amplification weakens: the tail realised $R^{2}$ is still clearly positive at the section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} setting ($\varphi=0.25$, $\mu=0.05$) but flat, between $-0.01$ and $0.04$, over the $\varphi\leq0.10$, $\mu\leq0.05$ block and the entire $\varphi=0.05$ row at every $\mu$ tested: with no signal to amplify (baseline realised $R^{2}$ of $-0.009$), the channel never activates even at the strongest price impact. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi = 0.25, mu = 0.05 cells span 0.119 to 0.131, lift about 0.075 over ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0]; phi <= 0.10, mu <= 0.05 block spans -0.006 to 0.040; phi = 0.05 row spans -0.006 to 0.027 at every mu, per-mu means over w = 0.0001, 0.014, 0.015, 0.017, 0.010; further near-flat cells outside the block: phi = 0.15, mu = 0.025 at w = 100 and 250 (0.028, 0.036; 0.042 at w = 500) and phi = 0.10, mu = 0.15, w = 100 (0.039), so 12 out-of-block cells sit at or below 0.04); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 row mean -0.009)
The lowest-$\varphi$ rows bound the erosion claim from below: at $\varphi\leq0.10$ the demand-adjusted baseline is itself near zero, leaving no signal to lose: the $\varphi=0.05$ row never crosses and the $\varphi=0.10$ row fires only at low shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 and 0.10 row means -0.013 and -0.003); ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (phi = 0.05 row all NaN; phi = 0.10 crossings 0.004 to 0.146); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb summary report cell (weak-signal boundary condition)
The boundary does not extend to the economic endpoint: adopter mean profit grows with adoption at every cost level tested (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}), so the erosion result concerns the independent signal, not profitability in this baseline. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 exceeds column 1 in every row); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb summary report cell (cost-free baseline shows no economic erosion)

\subsection{Limitations}

The findings carry two kinds of boundary: scope restrictions built into the deliberately minimal design, and implementation conventions that sections~\ref{sec:phase7-results}, \ref{sec:discussion-phi}, and \ref{sec:discussion-boundary} lean on directly.

\begin{itemize}
\item The market is single-asset, and linear price impact $\mu D_t$ is the only channel through which trading feeds back into returns; cross-asset and portfolio effects are out of scope, and non-linear impact, such as a square-root law, is not tested. % src: design scope per sec:model-market (linear impact the single feedback channel; single risky asset); CLAUDE.md "Do not add multiple assets"
\item The only forecasting rule studied is the rolling AR($p$); machine-learning and non-parametric forecasters are excluded by design. Time is discrete, so latency and other microstructure effects are not modelled. % src: sec:model-rules (penalised regressions, tree ensembles, recurrent architectures deliberately excluded); sec:model-market (discrete time)
\item Non-adopters are a homogeneous pool of random null traders; a heterogeneous ecology of trend followers and contrarians remains an unimplemented extension. Transaction costs are linear and symmetric, with no inventory or financing costs. % src: sec:model-rules (null rule); proposal v4 extensions list ("Heterogeneous trader ecology ... trend followers, contrarians, and fundamentalists") and CLAUDE.md ("A second optional extension is heterogeneous trader ecology ... if time allows"; only the transaction-cost extension was implemented in phase 7); sec:phase7-results (proportional cost c only)
\item All profits are marked at the post-impact quote and unwinds never re-enter the order flow, so every profit figure is a paper profit under the model's own accounting. In particular, the null benchmark books own-impact profit $\mu\sigma_q^{2}/N = 2.5\times10^{-4}$ per period on a position roughly 16 times the adopter cap, which dominates the null-relative endpoint at $c=0$ (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}). % src: sec:phase7-results; notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb closing accounting note and null-benchmark cell; phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['mu'] = 0.05, ['N'] = 200, sigma_q = 1.0 give 2.5e-4; ['null_mean_abs'] / ['q_cap'] = 15.96
\item The market maker absorbs position levels rather than position changes, the \citet{beja1980} convention rather than \citet{farmerjoshi2002} order flow, so a persistent position exerts persistent price pressure, strengthening the persistence mechanism of section~\ref{sec:discussion-phi}. % src: src/reflexive_market/market.py module docstring; sec:model-market implementation note
\item At the baseline risk scale and position cap the clip in \eqref{eq:advanced-order} binds in nearly every period, so adopters trade as fixed-size sign followers rather than graded mean-variance investors. Relatedly, the switching rule's risk-aversion coefficient ($a=1$) is set independently of the roughly 7 implied by the decision layer's risk scale at the realised return variance, keeping the certainty-equivalent penalty mild. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 ("the clip binds in nearly every period"); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['risk_scale'] = 0.001, ['q_cap'] = 0.05; phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_a'] = 1.0; notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (switching_a "set independently of risk_scale (which implies a ~ 7 at the realised return variance), so the penalty stays mild")
\end{itemize}
