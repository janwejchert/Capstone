{\Large\bfseries\color{accent} Abstract\par}
\vspace{0.3em}

\begin{minipage}{0.86\textwidth}
\justifying\small
This report studies how widespread adoption of a shared autoregressive forecasting rule affects forecast performance and economic value in a single-asset agent-based market. The market combines Brock-Hommes mean-variance demand with a Beja-Goldman / Farmer-Joshi market-maker price update. Traders may switch from a no-skill null rule to a public rolling AR forecast under either exogenous stochastic diffusion or endogenous certainty-equivalent switching. The forecast is evaluated against two distinct targets on the same path: the realised return, which is the metric a deployed forecaster actually sees, and a demand-adjusted counterfactual that strips out contemporaneous price impact.

The simulator identifies a dual-channel result. As adoption rises, the rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ against realised returns reaches roughly 0.19 at full adoption, against 0.07 for a zero-adoption control on the same shocks, through coordinated adopter demand. The same forecast's $R^{2}$ against the demand-adjusted return falls to roughly 0.04, against 0.08 under the control, with the rolling effective AR coefficient rising from 0.28 to 0.45 in step. Mean adopter profit is amplified rather than eroded in the cost-free baseline; the null-relative economic comparison is dominated by position-size asymmetry between the null rule and the capped adopter rule and is reported with that caveat. The headline summary is therefore that adoption raises realised predictive performance through a self-fulfilment channel while reducing the independent demand-adjusted signal the forecast was originally designed to exploit.
\end{minipage}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}

Consider a forecasting service that publishes a one-period-ahead return forecast for a single asset. At launch nobody trades on it, and its out-of-sample accuracy measures whatever genuine structure the rule has found. Then it acquires users: subscribers trade in the direction of the published signal, their coordinated orders move the price, and the returns against which the forecast is scored begin to carry its fingerprint. When the tracked performance metrics now shift, what exactly has changed: the forecast's skill, the market, or the meaning of the measurement? % src: framing per proposal v4 section 1 (deployed forecast with users whose trading feeds back into the evaluated returns); mechanism is mu D_t per \eqref{eq:return-law}

Both readings are defensible. The improvement could be real, since coordinated trading after a positive forecast pushes the realised return in the forecast's direction, so the forecast points where the price actually goes. It could equally be hollow, the independent regularity the rule was estimated to capture eroding under the very adoption that flatters the score. This report builds the smallest market in which that question can be posed, and answered, exactly. % src: the two readings are the report's two evaluation channels, sec:phase4-results; "smallest market" per the single-asset minimal design of sec:model

\subsection{Relation to previous work}

Three research traditions meet in this question. The first is reflexivity and its machine-learning formalisation, performative prediction: \citet{soros1987} argued that market participants' views alter the outcomes those views are about, and \citet{perdomo2020} made the deployed model's effect on its own evaluation distribution a formal object of study. The second is heterogeneous-agent finance, which supplies the market: mean-variance traders who switch between competing rules on recent performance, after \citet{brockhommes1998}, trading against a market maker who moves the price linearly in net order flow, in the disequilibrium tradition of \citet{beja1980} and \citet{farmerjoshi2002}. The third is classical out-of-sample forecast evaluation in the tradition of \citet{diebold1995}, whose machinery presumes a target that the forecast under test does not move, exactly the premise adoption breaks. The report joins the three: a performative forecast inside a heterogeneous-agent market, scored with standard out-of-sample metrics, with the divergence between evaluation targets as the central result. Section~\ref{sec:background} develops each strand. % src: positioning per proposal v4 sections 2.1 and 2.2 (literature strands); the exogenous-target premise restates sec 2.3 of this report ("the target is exogenous of the forecast"); diebold1995 entry added to references.bib per stage instructions

\subsection{Research question and contribution}

The research question is: how does adoption of a shared autoregressive return forecast affect its realised-return predictive performance, its demand-adjusted predictive performance, and its economic value to adopters, in a tractable single-asset agent-based market? % src: proposal v4 section 2.3 ("Core research question: How does adoption of a return-forecasting rule change realised market predictability, the independent demand-adjusted signal, and economic value in a reflexive single-asset market?")
The two performance readings score the same forecast on the same path against two targets: the realised return, price impact included, the only number a deployed forecaster can compute, and a demand-adjusted counterfactual with contemporaneous price impact stripped out. % src: proposal v4 section 3.6 (two evaluation channels); x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - mu D_t per \eqref{eq:decomposition}, contemporaneous impact only, not the full regression residual

The contribution is a small, fully reproducible simulator that demonstrates and quantifies a dual-channel answer. At the baseline configuration, adoption lifts the realised-return out-of-sample $R^{2}$ from 0.07 under a zero-adoption control to 0.19 at near-full adoption, while the demand-adjusted reading falls from 0.08 to 0.04 and measured return persistence rises from 0.28 to 0.45 (section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}). % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 2] = 0.0695 (control realised), [2, 3] = 0.1904 (fast regime, high-adoption window, mean A = 0.994 per [2, 1]); [0, 4] = 0.0821, [2, 5] = 0.0386 (demand-adjusted); [0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470 (effective AR coefficient)
A 3600-run sweep then maps both channels across price impact $\mu$, input persistence $\varphi$, estimation window $w$, and AR order $p$, locating the adoption shares at which the independent signal has lost half its low-adoption baseline (section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}). % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['mu_grid'] (5) x ['phi_grid'] (6) x ['w_grid'] (3) x ['forecast_p_grid'] (4) x ['num_seeds'] (10) = 3600 runs; ['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5 (half-baseline convention)
Mean adopter profit grows with adoption at every transaction-cost level tested, costs setting where the growth starts to pay rather than reversing it (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (high-adoption column exceeds low-adoption column in every cost row of c_grid = 0 to 0.01)

\section{Background and theoretical framework}\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Reflexivity and performative prediction}

The report rests on two framing ideas; the older is reflexivity. \citet{soros1987} argued that financial markets are not passive measurement devices: participants act on their views of the market, and through those actions alter the prices, and at times the fundamentals, that the views are about. Expectation and outcome are linked in both directions, so a view can come true partly because it is widely held, the self-fulfilling case, or undo itself by changing the conditions that once made it accurate. % src: soros1987 (reflexivity as two-way feedback between participants' views and the outcomes those views are about); proposal v4 section 2.1 (key term: reflexivity, "a forecast influences trading, trading influences returns, and returns then determine whether the forecast continues to perform well")

Machine learning reached the same structure from the deployment side. \citet{perdomo2020} formalise performative prediction: prediction problems in which the deployed model shifts the distribution of the data on which it is judged, so that measured accuracy is a property of the model and the behavioural response together, not of the model alone. Their central distinction, between models that are merely fixed points of retraining on the data they induce and models that remain optimal once the induced shift is accounted for, made the deployment effect an object of analysis in its own right. % src: perdomo2020 (performatively stable vs performatively optimal points; repeated risk minimisation converges to the stable ones); proposal v4 section 2.1 (key term: performative prediction, "accuracy can no longer be assessed as though the model were a passive observer")

This report sits at the join of the two ideas. A public return forecast that acquires users with price impact is reflexive in the first sense and performative in the second: the orders it coordinates move the market it describes. Once that happens, scoring the forecast with an off-the-shelf accuracy metric no longer measures forecast skill alone; the score mixes skill with whatever part of the outcome the forecast's own users produced, and the two parts need not move in the same direction. The market of section~\ref{sec:model} makes the feedback channel a single explicit term, so the mixture can be taken apart exactly. % src: proposal v4 section 2.1 ("performative effects can move different evaluation targets in opposite directions"); the explicit channel is mu D_t per \eqref{eq:return-law}

\subsection{Heterogeneous-agent finance and price formation}

The demand side of the model comes from heterogeneous-agent finance, in which market dynamics are generated by interacting traders using different, competing rules. The reference framework is \citet{brockhommes1998}: each trader is a myopic mean-variance maximiser with constant absolute risk aversion, holding a position proportional to the expected next-period return and inversely proportional to risk aversion times perceived variance, and traders switch between forecasting rules through a discrete-choice mechanism driven by recent relative performance. Coupling rule choice to rule performance this way can destabilise an otherwise simple asset market, the routes to chaos of their title. The present design takes from the framework the zero-dividend special case of the demand function and the binary discrete-choice switch, applied to one shared forecasting rule against a no-skill null (section~\ref{sec:model-traders}). % src: brockhommes1998 (CARA mean-variance demand; logit discrete-choice switching on past performance; bifurcations as the intensity of choice rises); proposal v4 sections 2.2 (second strand) and 3.1.2

Price formation comes from the disequilibrium market-adjustment tradition. \citet{beja1980} study prices that respond to excess demand at a finite rate instead of clearing every period, so trading pressure moves the quote directly, and show that introducing trend-chasing speculation into such a market can destabilise it. \citet{farmerjoshi2002} cast the same idea in discrete time as a market maker who absorbs net order flow and shifts the log-price linearly in it, and show that common trading strategies leave statistical signatures in returns, trend following in particular inducing positive short-horizon autocorrelation. Both results carry weight here: linear impact is the only channel through which forecast use feeds back into returns, and the induced autocorrelation licenses the small reduced-form AR component the shared rule is built to recover (section~\ref{sec:model-market}). % src: beja1980 (finite-speed price adjustment to excess demand; destabilising trend-based speculation); farmerjoshi2002 (log-linear market-maker price update, their eq. 7; trend following induces positive first-lag return autocorrelation, their eqs. 8 to 11); proposal v4 sections 2.2 (second strand) and 3.1.4 to 3.1.5

\subsection{Forecast evaluation under self-fulfilment}

Out-of-sample forecast evaluation has a settled routine: fit on data through period $t$, forecast period $t+1$, accumulate mean squared forecast error, and summarise the result against a benchmark as an out-of-sample $R^{2}$. The routine's unstated premise is that the target is exogenous of the forecast: the return being predicted would have been the same had the forecast never been issued, so the score measures the forecast and nothing else. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1 (MSFE and out-of-sample R^2 as the standard endpoint pair)

Adoption removes that premise. Once traders act on a public forecast, their coordinated orders enter the realised return through price impact, so the target partially inherits the forecast it is supposed to test. A single off-the-shelf score then mixes two channels, the independent signal the rule was estimated to capture and the self-fulfilment its users inject, and because the two carry opposite signs in this setting, the score can improve while the independent content behind it decays. No refinement of estimation windows, benchmarks, or test statistics repairs this, because the defect sits in the target, not in the procedure. % src: proposal v4 sections 2.1 (endogenous concept drift) and 3.6 (two evaluation channels with opposite expected directions)

The response of this design is a decomposition rather than a new metric: the same forecast is scored on the same path against two targets, the realised return and a demand-adjusted counterfactual with contemporaneous price impact stripped out, and the wedge between the two readings is attributed to deployment rather than skill. Section~\ref{sec:metrics} defines the resulting endpoints exactly; section~\ref{sec:discussion-interpretation} reads the simulated findings back through this lens. % src: proposal v4 sections 3.1.5 (decomposition, eq. 7) and 4.1 (two R^2 readings on equal footing)

Four terms carry the argument. The \emph{adoption share} is the fraction of traders currently trading on the public forecast, moved either by exogenous diffusion at rate $\pi$ or by endogenous performance-based switching (section~\ref{sec:model-adoption}). The \emph{realised return} is the return the market actually prints, contemporaneous price impact included: the only target a deployed forecaster can observe. The \emph{demand-adjusted return} $x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - \mu D_t$ is its counterfactual companion, removing the contemporaneous price-impact term only while leaving the lagged-return component in place; it is not the full residual of a return regression, and it asks what the forecast would have scored had its users not moved the current quote (section~\ref{sec:model-decomposition}). The \emph{effective AR coefficient} is the lag-1 autocorrelation of realised returns in a trailing window: it sits at the input $\varphi$ without adoption, rises above it when coordinated adopter demand reinforces the lagged-return component, and is the diagnostic connecting the two $R^{2}$ readings (section~\ref{sec:metrics-phi}). % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (x_{t+1} removes mu D_t only; the full regression residual would be sigma eps = r_{t+1} - phi r_t - mu D_t) and section 4.1.3 (effective AR coefficient at the input phi without adoption, rising under adoption); A_t = (1/N) sum_i a_{i,t} per section sec:model-adoption


\section{The model}\label{sec:model}

\subsection{Single-asset market with linear price impact}\label{sec:model-market}

The market is deliberately minimal. Time is discrete, $t = 0, 1, \ldots, T$. A single risky asset trades at log-price $p_t$ with one-period return $r_{t+1} = p_{t+1} - p_t$; the risk-free rate is normalised to zero and the asset pays no dividend, so $r_{t+1}$ is an excess return. Each of $N$ traders submits a signed order $q_{i,t}$ every period. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.1 (asset and agents)
There is no Walrasian auctioneer: following the disequilibrium market-adjustment tradition, a risk-neutral market maker absorbs whatever aggregate order flow arrives and shifts the log-price in proportion to it \citep{beja1980,farmerjoshi2002}. Linear price impact is thus the single channel through which trading feeds back into returns, the carrier of every reflexive effect this report studies. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1 (Baseline market environment), eqs. (4)-(6)

Aggregate signed demand is the per-trader average order,
\begin{equation}
  D_t \;=\; \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} q_{i,t},
  \label{eq:aggregate-demand}
\end{equation}
and the market maker updates the log-price according to
\begin{equation}
  p_{t+1} \;=\; p_t \;+\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad \varepsilon_{t+1} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1),
  \label{eq:price-update}
\end{equation}
where the price-impact coefficient $\mu = 1/\lambda > 0$ is the reciprocal of the market maker's liquidity $\lambda$ and $\sigma > 0$ scales the exogenous news shock; \eqref{eq:price-update} is the zero-fundamental special case of the Farmer-Joshi log-linear price update. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.4 (eqs. 4 and 5; "zero-fundamental specialisation of Farmer and Joshi (2002, Eq. 7)"); liquidity reading of lambda per farmerjoshi2002 (discussion of their eq. 7)
First-differencing \eqref{eq:price-update} gives a one-period market-maker return of $\mu D_t + \sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$; the baseline return law adds an autoregressive component carried in from prior periods,
\begin{equation}
  r_{t+1} \;=\; \varphi\, r_t \;+\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}, \qquad |\varphi| < 1\ \text{small.}
  \label{eq:return-law}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:return-law} splits the return into the three pieces the model works with: a small predictable component $\varphi r_t$, the signal the rolling AR rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} estimates; the contemporaneous price impact $\mu D_t$, the only term through which trader behaviour enters; and unpredictable news $\sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$. The $\varphi r_t$ term is not ad hoc: \citet{farmerjoshi2002} show that linear trend-following demand induces positive first-lag return autocorrelation of the same order as its intensity, which \eqref{eq:return-law} absorbs as a single reduced-form coefficient rather than a separate trader type. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 6; Farmer and Joshi (2002, Eqs. 8 to 11), rho_r(1) = alpha/(1+alpha))

One implementation choice deserves note: where \citet{farmerjoshi2002} have the market maker absorb net order flow (changes in positions), \texttt{market.py} absorbs the level of aggregate demand each period, closer to the Beja-Goldman excess-demand adjustment, so a persistent position exerts persistent price pressure; consistently, the later profit and cost accounting prices a full position turnover every period. % src: src/reflexive_market/market.py module docstring

\subsection{Trader behaviour: belief, decision, and switching layers}\label{sec:model-traders}

Trader behaviour decomposes into three layers, kept separate so the results can say where each channel of the dual-channel result is produced: belief (what return the agent expects), decision (how that belief becomes a position), and switching (whether the agent uses the shared rule at all). The mechanism under study operates on the belief layer, where the rolling AR forecast is public information, and reaches returns only through the decision and switching layers, where action is private. The belief layer has exactly two states: an adopter's belief is the shared rolling autoregressive forecast $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ of section~\ref{sec:model-rules}, identical across adopters because the rule is public, while a non-adopter holds no belief about returns at all, the null rule of the same section generating order flow that is directionally uninformative on average, an active but skill-free benchmark rather than a behavioural model. % src: proposal v4 section 3.2 (A three-layer model of trader behaviour); section 3.3 (null rule, "directionally uninformative on average")

The decision layer follows \citet{brockhommes1998}: each trader is a myopic mean-variance maximiser with constant absolute risk aversion. With conditional forecast $\hat{r}_{i,t+1}$ of the next-period return and common perceived conditional variance $\sigma^{2} > 0$, the optimal signed position is % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.2 (Trader demand, eq. 1)
\begin{equation}
  z_{i,t} \;=\; \frac{\hat{r}_{i,t+1}}{a\, \sigma^{2}},
  \label{eq:bh-demand}
\end{equation}
where $a > 0$ is the coefficient of absolute risk aversion; this is the zero-dividend, zero-rate special case of the Brock-Hommes demand function: buy on a positive expected return, sell on a negative one, trade smaller when the market is perceived as riskier. Adopters evaluate \eqref{eq:bh-demand} at the public forecast, with the result clipped at a position cap, % src: proposal v4 sections 3.1.2 (economic meaning of eq. 1) and 3.1.3 (eq. 3)
\begin{equation}
  q_{i,t}^{(A)} \;=\; \mathrm{clip}\!\left(\frac{\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}}{a\, \sigma^{2}},\; -\bar{q}_{i},\; +\bar{q}_{i}\right),
  \label{eq:advanced-order}
\end{equation}
where the cap $\bar{q}_{i} > 0$ is a leverage or inventory constraint: rolling-window estimates of $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ are noisy, and the cap prevents a single extreme forecast from generating an unboundedly large trade. Non-adopters bypass the demand mapping entirely; their null order enters as drawn. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.3 (role of the position cap)

The switching layer is a single binary state: $a_{i,t} \in \{0, 1\}$ indicates whether trader $i$ has adopted the shared rule at time $t$. The final order is $q_{i,t} = (1 - a_{i,t})\, q_{i,t}^{(0)} + a_{i,t}\, q_{i,t}^{(A)}$, and the one-period profit feeding both the economic endpoint and the performance-based switching rule is $\Pi_{i,t+1} = q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$; how $a_{i,t}$ evolves, by stochastic diffusion or certainty-equivalent comparison, is the subject of section~\ref{sec:model-adoption}. Two details from \texttt{traders.py} matter later. First, only the product $a\sigma^{2}$ enters the demand mapping, so the simulator passes it as a single risk-scale parameter. Second, the advanced order returns zero whenever the forecast is not finite, so the warm-up periods before the first rolling window fills leave adopters inactive rather than injecting undefined demand. % src: proposal v4 sections 3.1.3 (eq. 2) and 3.1.6 (eq. 8); src/reflexive_market/traders.py advanced_order docstring (risk_scale, non-finite forecast guard); src/reflexive_market/simulate.py main loop (orders = (1 - adoption) * null + adoption * q_adv)

\subsection{Null rule and rolling AR forecasting rule}\label{sec:model-rules}

Non-adopters trade by the null rule: trader $i$ submits $q_{i,t}^{(0)} = \xi_{i,t}$ with $\xi_{i,t} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_{q}^{2})$, drawn independently across traders and time. The rule is a reference point, not a model of real trading: it keeps every trader active while injecting zero-mean, directionally uninformative order flow, so any systematic predictability or profitability the advanced rule generates is measured against a market in which trading happens but is not forecast-driven. One design feature matters downstream: the null order bypasses the demand mapping and carries no position cap, entering aggregate demand as drawn with typical magnitude set by $\sigma_q$ alone, while the advanced order \eqref{eq:advanced-order} is clipped at $\bar{q}_{i}$. This position-size asymmetry returns in section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}, where it dominates the null-relative economic endpoint in both directions. % src: proposal v4 section 3.3 (Null model of trading, eq. 9; "no-skill random trading rule", "directionally uninformative on average"); src/reflexive_market/traders.py null_orders (draws returned unclipped; the clip in eq. (3) applies only in advanced_order)

Adopters share a single advanced rule: an autoregressive forecast of the next return, estimated on a rolling window of recent returns. With rolling-window length $w$ and autoregressive order $p$, at time $t$ the public model fits by least squares % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 (Advanced forecasting rule, eqs. 10 and 11)
\begin{equation}
  r_{s+1} \;=\; c \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \varphi_{j}\, r_{s+1-j} \;+\; u_{s+1},
  \label{eq:ar-fit}
\end{equation}
using only the observations in the window $s = t - w, \ldots, t - 1$, and issues the one-step-ahead out-of-sample forecast
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}(w, p) \;=\; \hat{c}_{t, w, p} \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{p} \hat{\varphi}_{j, t, w, p}\, r_{t+1-j}.
  \label{eq:ar-forecast}
\end{equation}
Nothing estimated at time $t$ uses information from after $t$, so the forecast is out of sample by construction; rolling estimation lets the coefficients adapt when the return process changes, which under adoption it does, the fit of \eqref{eq:ar-fit} being the object that later absorbs the amplified empirical autocorrelation of realised returns. % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 ("using only information that would have been available at time t"; "Rolling estimation allows the coefficients to adapt"); section 4.1.2 (rolling fit picks up an amplified empirical autocorrelation)
The rule is built to recover the $\varphi r_t$ component of \eqref{eq:return-law}, the simplest forecasting environment that should in principle work; penalised regressions, tree ensembles, and recurrent architectures are deliberately excluded. \texttt{forecast.py} implements the fit as ordinary least squares on a lagged design with intercept, defaulting to $p = 1$; the first $w$ forecasts are NaN, the warm-up the non-finite-forecast guard converts into adopter inactivity. The core design holds $p = 1$ with windows $w \in \{50, 100, 250\}$; higher orders $p \in \{2, 5, 10\}$ enter only as a phase 6 robustness check. % src: proposal v4 section 3.4 (purpose of the baseline, excluded model classes, w in {50, 100, 250}, AR(1) core); section 3.7 (higher-order AR rule, p in {2, 5, 10}, phase 6 robustness); src/reflexive_market/forecast.py rolling_ar_forecast (np.linalg.lstsq via fit_ar, p=1 default, first window entries NaN)

\subsection{Adoption mechanisms}\label{sec:model-adoption}

Two mechanisms govern how the adoption indicator $a_{i,t}$ evolves; both act on the switching layer alone, and the simulator runs exactly one at a time. The stochastic mechanism, easier to interpret, asks whether wider use of the forecast is by itself enough to change predictability; the performance-based mechanism studies the feedback between early success and later crowding. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5 ("Two mechanisms are considered ... Both act on the switching layer of section 3.2"; closing paragraph on implementation order); src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (ValueError when diffusion and switching are both active)

\subsubsection{Stochastic diffusion}

Under stochastic diffusion each non-adopter discovers and adopts the public rule with a fixed probability $\pi \in (0, 1)$ per period, and each adopter abandons it with an optional small exit probability $\delta$: % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.1 (Stochastic diffusion, eqs. 12 and 13)
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 1 \mid a_{i,t} = 0) \;=\; \pi,
  \label{eq:diffusion-entry}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 0 \mid a_{i,t} = 1) \;=\; \delta.
  \label{eq:diffusion-exit}
\end{equation}
The draws are independent across agents and periods, so the adoption share $A_t = (1/N)\sum_i a_{i,t}$ moves at an expected per-period rate of $\pi(1 - A_t) - \delta A_t$, with no social learning and no profitability comparison: adoption is imposed on the market rather than generated by it, isolating the pure effect of wider use of a common signal. % src: src/reflexive_market/adoption.py stochastic_diffusion_step (independent per-agent transitions); proposal v4 section 3.5.1 ("without explicitly modelling social learning or profitability comparisons", "isolating the pure effect of wider use of a common signal"); expected rate follows from eqs. (12) and (13) by counting expected entries pi(1-A_t) and exits delta A_t
One implementation detail matters for every later comparison: adoption draws come from a dedicated random generator, so changing $\pi$ or $\delta$ cannot advance the market shock stream, and regimes with different adoption settings run on paired shocks. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (adoption_rng spawn comment: no-op adoption settings leave the realised market path identical; "across-regime comparisons stay paired on the same shocks")

\subsubsection{Certainty-equivalent performance-based switching}

The economically meaningful mechanism makes entry depend on recent relative risk-adjusted success, scored in the same mean-variance terms as the decision layer. Each rule $k \in \{(0), (A)\}$ carries a one-period certainty-equivalent return, % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (Risk-adjusted performance-based switching; "the switching score is constructed to be consistent with that preference structure")
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{CE}_t^{(k)} \;=\; \bar{\Pi}_t^{(k)} \;-\; \tfrac{a}{2}\, \hat{\sigma}_{t}^{2,(k)},
  \label{eq:ce}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\Pi}_t^{(k)}$ and $\hat{\sigma}_{t}^{2,(k)}$ are the rolling mean and variance of the rule's per-period profit over a window of length $W_A$, and $a$ is the risk-aversion coefficient of the decision layer. The switching score is the gap $S_t = \mathrm{CE}_t^{(A)} - \mathrm{CE}_t^{(0)}$, and each non-adopter switches to the advanced rule with the logistic probability % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 14 and the inline definition of S_t)
\begin{equation}
  P(a_{i,t+1} = 1 \mid a_{i,t} = 0) \;=\; \Lambda(\alpha + \beta\, S_t), \qquad \Lambda(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}},
  \label{eq:logit-switch}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is a baseline adoption term and $\beta > 0$ controls sensitivity to recent performance; the specification is the binary \citet{brockhommes1998} discrete-choice mechanism applied at the switching layer. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 15 and surrounding prose, "binary BH98 discrete-choice mechanism")
Two implementation choices complete the rule. First, the design specifies an entry probability but no exit analogue of \eqref{eq:diffusion-exit}, and \texttt{adoption.py} keeps that asymmetry: adopters retain their state, so the share can only ratchet upward. % src: src/reflexive_market/adoption.py performance_switching_step docstring ("The proposal specifies only the entry direction ... Adopters keep their state. This matches the asymmetric form used by stochastic diffusion") and code (entry-only update, no exit branch); proposal v4 section 3.5.2 (eq. 15 conditions on a_{i,t} = 0 only)
Second, the profit streams feeding \eqref{eq:ce} belong to one representative trader per rule, the common capped adopter order and the realised order of a single representative non-adopter, keeping the per-trader mean and variance of null profit independent of the adoption share. % src: src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (representative-profit comment block; null representative is the lowest-indexed non-adopter)
Unlike stochastic diffusion, this mechanism closes a second reflexive loop: early adopter success raises $\mathrm{CE}_t^{(A)}$ and accelerates entry, and the entrants' own trading reshapes the returns from which every later certainty equivalent is computed. % src: proposal v4 section 3.5 closing paragraph ("feedback between early success and later crowding")

\subsection{Intra-period timing}\label{sec:model-timing}

The preceding equations fix what each trader and the market maker do, but not when within a period they do it. The positive-sign impact term $\mu D_t$ in \eqref{eq:return-law} makes that order of operations load-bearing: adopter demand formed on today's forecast moves today's quote, so forecast and evaluation target are entangled within the period, and self-fulfilment cannot be told from erosion unless the sequence of events is explicit. The design fixes the following sequence inside each period, and the simulator's single state-evolution loop, \texttt{run()} in \texttt{simulate.py}, follows it exactly. % src: proposal v4 section 3.6 (Intra-period timing and the two evaluation channels), opening paragraph; src/reflexive_market/simulate.py module docstring (loop follows the section 3.6 timing)

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
\item Agents observe returns up to and including period $t$ and form forecasts $\hat{r}_{t+1}^{(A)}$ per \eqref{eq:ar-forecast}.
\item Adopters and non-adopters submit orders $q_{i,t}$: the advanced order \eqref{eq:advanced-order} and the null order, combined through the adoption indicator $a_{i,t}$ as in section~\ref{sec:model-traders}.
\item The market maker absorbs aggregate demand $D_t$ and moves the quote immediately, as in the price update \eqref{eq:price-update}.
\item The exogenous news shock $\sigma\,\varepsilon_{t+1}$ and the autoregressive term $\varphi\, r_t$ realise.
\end{enumerate}
% src: proposal v4 section 3.6, four-step sequence (its references to proposal eqs. (2), (3), (5) map to proposal eq. (2) inline, \eqref{eq:advanced-order}, \eqref{eq:price-update}); CLAUDE.md "Intra-period timing" steps 1 to 4 (identical substance and order)

The sequence separates what the forecast knows from what its users cause: returns through period $t$ are in hand before any order is placed, and the $\mu D_t$ move of step 3 is in the books before next period's news arrives in step 4, making it well defined to score the same forecast against two targets on the same path, the realised return including the step 3 impact or a counterfactual stripping it out (next subsection). % src: proposal v4 section 3.6 ("Given this timing, the forecast can be evaluated against either of two distinct targets")

\subsection{The realised vs demand-adjusted decomposition}\label{sec:model-decomposition}

For evaluation the return law \eqref{eq:return-law} is rewritten as a contemporaneous price-impact component plus an ex-feedback remainder: the realised return splits as % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 7; "a decomposition into a contemporaneous price-impact component and an ex-feedback remainder")
\begin{equation}
  r_{t+1} \;=\; \mu\, D_t \;+\; x_{t+1},
  \label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
  x_{t+1} \;=\; r_{t+1} \;-\; \mu\, D_t \;=\; \varphi\, r_t \;+\; \sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1}
  \label{eq:demand-adjusted}
\end{equation}
is the demand-adjusted return, the part of the period-$(t+1)$ return that does not depend on contemporaneous adopter trading: the lagged-return component and the exogenous news shock. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 (eq. 7; "the part of the return that does not depend on contemporaneous adopter trading"; the x = r - mu D form is from the "What x_{t+1} is and is not" paragraph)
The decomposition matters because the rolling AR rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} is, by construction, an estimate of the AR component of the return process and therefore of $x_{t+1}$ in expectation, while the only return a deployed forecaster can observe and score against is $r_{t+1}$. Both targets are therefore primary endpoints with asymmetric interpretations. Realised-return $R^{2}$ scores the forecast against $r_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:decomposition}: the total performance a deployed forecaster actually sees, including whatever part of the return coordinated adopter orders have pushed in the forecast direction through $\mu D_t$, the reading expected to rise with adoption (the self-fulfilling channel). Demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ scores the same forecast against $x_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:demand-adjusted}: the counterfactual that strips out the contemporaneous price impact and asks whether the independent signal the forecast was designed to exploit survives once self-fulfilment is removed, the reading expected to fall (the demand-adjusted-erosion channel). % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 ("an estimate of the AR component of the return process and therefore an estimate of x_{t+1} in expectation"; "realised-return R^2 as the metric a deployed forecaster actually sees and demand-adjusted R^2 as the counterfactual that asks whether the independent signal survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out"; "adopter orders coordinated by the public signal push the realised return in the forecast direction") and section 3.6 (channel names and expected directions)

One definitional caveat is worth stating exactly. The demand-adjusted return is \emph{not} the full regression residual of an AR(1) fit on returns: under the data-generating process \eqref{eq:return-law}, that residual is the pure exogenous news shock, $\sigma\, \varepsilon_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - \varphi\, r_t - \mu\, D_t$. The quantity $x_{t+1}$ removes the contemporaneous adopter-demand term $\mu D_t$ only, leaving the lagged-return persistence channel $\varphi r_t$ in place. It is best read as a counterfactual: the return path the market would have produced had adopter demand not moved the quote in the current period, so that the predictable AR(1) component still has a chance to show up in the next-period observation. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5, paragraph "What x_{t+1} is and is not" (residual formula sigma eps_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - phi r_t - mu D_t and counterfactual reading, near-verbatim); CLAUDE.md overview ("it is *not* the full regression residual, which would be sigma eps = r_{t+1} - phi r_t - mu D_t")
The distinction names the erosion channel: against $\sigma\varepsilon_{t+1}$, independent of everything known at time $t$, any forecast carries zero signal in expectation whatever adoption does, while $x_{t+1}$ retains exactly the predictable component the forecast was built to recover, so a fall in demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ records the erosion of an independent signal that was genuinely there. That is the claim ``demand-adjusted erosion'' makes, and the reading tracked from section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} onward. % src: proposal v4 section 3.1.5 ("The counterfactual is what isolates the independent predictive signal that the forecast was originally designed to exploit"); eps_{t+1} iid N(0,1) per proposal eq. (5), reproduced as \eqref{eq:price-update}; channel name per proposal section 3.6 ("Demand-adjusted-erosion channel")


\section{Metrics and experimental design}\label{sec:metrics}

\subsection{Primary forecast-performance endpoints}

\subsubsection{Realised-return $R^{2}$}\label{sec:metrics-r2-realised}

The first primary endpoint is the standard out-of-sample $R^{2}$ of the one-step forecast \eqref{eq:ar-forecast} against the realised return, the target a deployed forecaster can observe:
\begin{equation}
  R^{2}_{\mathrm{realised}}(t) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(r_{s+1} - \hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}\bigr)^{2}}{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(r_{s+1} - \bar{r}\bigr)^{2}},
  \label{eq:r2-realised}
\end{equation}
where the sums run over the most recent $W$ forecast-target pairs ending at period $t$ and $\bar{r}$ is the mean realised return over that window. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 (R^2_realised display, "computed in a trailing window ending at t"); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2
The evaluation window is distinct from the AR estimation window $w$; every reported run uses $W = 1000$ periods, the notebooks' \texttt{eval\_window} parameter. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['eval_window'] = phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['eval_window'] = 1000

The forecasts entering \eqref{eq:r2-realised} are strictly out of sample, each $\hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}$ fitted on returns through period $s$ only. One convention is worth stating once: \texttt{rolling\_oos\_r2} in \texttt{metrics.py} benchmarks against the trailing window's in-sample mean of the target, not a real-time prevailing mean. The choice slightly favours the benchmark, and with near-zero-mean returns the difference is negligible. % src: src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2 docstring ("The benchmark is the trailing window's in-sample mean of the target, not a real-time prevailing mean, which slightly favours the benchmark. With near-zero-mean returns the difference is negligible. The forecasts themselves are strictly out of sample.")
Because the target contains the contemporaneous impact term $\mu D_s$ of \eqref{eq:decomposition}, this is the total-performance reading of section~\ref{sec:model-decomposition}, expected to rise with adoption through the self-fulfilling channel. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 ("expected to rise with adoption rather than fall, because coordinated adopter trading pushes r_{t+1} in the forecast direction")

\subsubsection{Demand-adjusted-return $R^{2}$}

The second primary endpoint scores the same forecast, on the same path and trailing window, against the demand-adjusted return $x_{s+1}$ of \eqref{eq:demand-adjusted}:
\begin{equation}
  R^{2}_{\mathrm{da}}(t) \;=\; 1 \;-\; \frac{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(x_{s+1} - \hat{r}^{(A)}_{s+1}\bigr)^{2}}{\sum_{s=t-W}^{t-1} \bigl(x_{s+1} - \bar{x}\bigr)^{2}},
  \label{eq:r2-da}
\end{equation}
with $\bar{x}$ the window mean of the demand-adjusted return; the benchmark convention of section~\ref{sec:metrics-r2-realised} applies unchanged. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.2 (R^2_da display with x_{s+1} = r_{s+1} - mu D_s); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2 applied to the demand-adjusted target, e.g. notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb metrics cell (rolling_oos_r2(demand_adjusted, forecasts, eval_window))
The same forecast can earn two different $R^{2}$ readings on the same path because the targets differ by exactly that impact term: adopter orders are aligned with the forecast, so $\mu D_s$ systematically moves the realised target toward the forecast, while a rolling fit that has absorbed the resulting amplified autocorrelation over-predicts the demand-adjusted target. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.1 ("coordinated adopter trading pushes r_{t+1} in the forecast direction") and section 4.1.2 ("the rolling fit picks up an amplified empirical autocorrelation from realised returns and over-predicts the demand-adjusted process")
The two endpoints are reported on equal footing, neither being canonical: $R^{2}_{\mathrm{realised}}$ measures what a deployed forecaster sees, and $R^{2}_{\mathrm{da}}$ whether the independent signal the forecast was built to exploit survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out, the erosion channel whose thresholds section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} defines. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.2 ("The two R^2 readings are reported on equal footing; neither is the canonical evaluation target, and each answers a different question") and section 4 intro (realised reading is the total performance a deployed forecaster sees; demand-adjusted reading is the counterfactual that tests whether the independent signal survives once self-fulfilment is stripped out)

\subsection{Primary economic endpoint}

The primary economic endpoint is net trading value: whether the forecast still pays once trading is costly. The transaction-cost extension charges a proportional cost $c$ per unit traded, turning the gross profit $\Pi_{i,t+1} = q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$ of section~\ref{sec:model-traders} into a net figure read against the null rule rather than against zero: per period, % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (net trading value, "net profit after transaction costs, reported both in absolute terms and relative to the null benchmark"; null-relative is "reported as the primary economic measure"); section 3.7 transaction-costs bullet ("Subtract proportional trading costs from profits to evaluate whether modest statistical predictability survives economically"); section 3.1.6 (profit eq. 8; "Transaction costs are omitted in the baseline and added as an extension in section 3.7")
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\Pi_{t+1} \;=\; \bigl(q^{(A)}_{t}\, r_{t+1} \;-\; c\,\lvert q^{(A)}_{t}\rvert\bigr) \;-\; \bigl(q^{(0)}_{t}\, r_{t+1} \;-\; c\, E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert\bigr),
  \label{eq:null-relative-profit}
\end{equation}
the adopter's net profit minus the null's, the latter a representative null trader's realised gross profit less its expected cost bill, with $q^{(A)}_{t}$ the common adopter order of \eqref{eq:advanced-order} and $E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert = \sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_{q}$ the null rule's mean absolute position. The rule pays where the mean of \eqref{eq:null-relative-profit} is positive, beating an active but skill-free benchmark net of both rules' costs. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (adopter net profit q^A r - c|q^A| minus a null benchmark accounting for "the null trader's realised gross P&L and its own per-period cost c E[|q^(0)|]"); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell (net_null[t] = null_profit[t] - c * sqrt(2/pi) * sigma_q) and run cell ("q_adv is the common per-period adopter order exported by the simulator"); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.7979 = sqrt(2/pi) * sigma_q at sigma_q = 1.0

Two properties of the benchmark shape this reading in section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}. First, the null's gross profit has positive mean $\mu\sigma_{q}^{2}/N$, $2.5\times10^{-4}$ at the phase 7 parameters: a null trader's own order moves the within-period quote, booking own-impact paper profit. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell comment ("E[null_profit] = mu sigma_q^2 / N (= 2.5e-4 at these parameters), own-impact paper profit"); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['mu'] = 0.05, ['N'] = 200, sigma_q = 1.0 (notebook parameters cell): 0.05 * 1.0 / 200 = 2.5e-4; src/reflexive_market/market.py aggregate_demand (D_t = (1/N) sum_i q_{i,t})
Second, both legs of the comparison scale with the null's position, whose mean absolute size, about $0.80$, is roughly 16 times the adopter cap $\bar{q} = 0.05$. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.798, ['q_cap'] = 0.05, ratio = 15.96; notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb null-benchmark cell ("a position with E|q^(0)| ~ 0.8, roughly 16x the adopter cap")
The design nevertheless treats the null-relative view as primary, since absolute profit does not control for the cost a no-skill rule itself pays whenever $c > 0$; phase 7 reports it accordingly, with the caveat that position-size asymmetry, not forecast content, dominates the comparison in both cost regimes. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.4 (absolute net profit secondary, "does not control for the cost a null rule itself pays in any cost regime c > 0"); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb done-when cell (null-relative endpoint "dominated by position-size asymmetry in both directions": below the benchmark at c = 0, above it at every c > 0); CLAUDE.md overview ("phase 7 reports it with that caveat")

\subsection{Effective AR coefficient as a market-dynamics diagnostic}\label{sec:metrics-phi}

The two $R^{2}$ endpoints score the forecast; the rolling effective AR coefficient $\hat{\varphi}^{\mathrm{eff}}(t)$ instead tracks the dynamics of the market the forecast trades in. Estimated from realised returns alone, with no reference to the forecast, \texttt{rolling\_phi} in \texttt{metrics.py} computes at each period the lag-1 sample autocorrelation of the most recent $W$ realised returns, with the same trailing $W = 1000$ as the $R^{2}$ endpoints in every reported run. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("estimated directly from the realised return series in a trailing window"); src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_phi (np.corrcoef(seg[:-1], seg[1:]) over the most recent `window` returns ending at each index; first window-1 entries NaN); window = 1000 everywhere: phase_02_benchmark.npz['rolling_window'] = 1000, phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['eval_window'] = phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['eval_window'] = 1000, each passed to rolling_phi in the corresponding notebook
With no adoption it should sit at the input $\varphi$ of the return law \eqref{eq:return-law}; section~\ref{sec:phases12-results} verifies that it does, with no drift. % src: phase_02_benchmark.npz['mean_phi_trace'] (flat at the input; total range 0.0156 over the periods with a complete window)
Under adoption it should rise above the input value: adopter demand responds positively to recent returns through the shared forecast, so the impact term $\mu D_t$ reinforces the lagged-return component and measured persistence grows, the mechanism behind the realised-$R^{2}$ climb of section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("adopter buying on a positive AR forecast reinforces the lagged-return component through mu D_t, so phi-eff is expected to rise with adoption")

It is a diagnostic rather than a third endpoint because its role is explanatory: the over-prediction mechanism of the previous subsection runs through this absorbed amplification, so the rise of $\hat{\varphi}^{\mathrm{eff}}$ is the mechanical reason the two $R^{2}$ readings diverge. % src: proposal v4 section 4 intro ("The remaining quantities are diagnostics, including the effective AR coefficient as a market-dynamics diagnostic that explains why the two R^2 readings move in opposite directions") and section 4.1.3 ("a rolling fit that has absorbed the amplified empirical autocorrelation will systematically over-predict against x_{t+1}")
Sections~\ref{sec:phase4-results} and~\ref{sec:phase5-results} track it against the adoption share, and the relative threshold $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$ of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} summarises its rise across the parameter grid of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5; report line "A*_{R2,da,rel} and A*_{phi,rel}, defined in section sec:metrics-a-star" (phased-construction list, phase 6 item)

The relative thresholds make erosion detectable in that regime and were fixed before the sweep was run: a fall to half the metric's own low-adoption baseline on the two $R^{2}$ targets, a rise to 1.5 times baseline on the effective coefficient, each defined only where that baseline is positive and flagged by the rel subscript so it is never mistaken for a zero crossing. % src: proposal v4 section 4.3 bullet 2 ("pre-registered relative threshold", reworded per the internal-only rule; "labelled A*_{R2,da,rel} to keep it distinct from the zero-crossing definition"); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5, ['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cells 0 and 3 (a_star NaN when the baseline is not positive)
The economic target runs the other way: transaction costs hand net profit a genuine zero to cross, so section~\ref{sec:phase7-results} reads both profit conventions against zero directly and no relative version is needed. % src: notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 0 ("Once transaction costs land in phase 7 the absolute zero-crossing definition becomes operational on the economic endpoint A*_{profit} and we report that one against zero, not as a relative threshold")

\section{Implementation}\label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{Software architecture}\label{sec:software-architecture}

The simulator is a small pure-numpy Python package, \texttt{reflexive\_market}, under \texttt{src/}: six working modules plus a seven-line \texttt{\_\_init\_\_.py} holding only a docstring and the version string, 588 lines in total. % src: wc -l src/reflexive_market/*.py (7+47+75+30+150+249+30 = 588)
Four design constraints, fixed in the project working agreement before any simulator code was written, shaped the package. % src: CLAUDE.md, Code conventions section (lines 203-209); scaffold commit 52b885a adds CLAUDE.md with "No simulator or notebook code yet"
First, the mathematics uses numpy alone: AR estimation goes through \texttt{np.linalg.lstsq}, and no pandas, scipy, statsmodels, or scikit-learn import appears anywhere in \texttt{src/}. Second, every random draw flows through a \texttt{numpy.random.Generator} passed in as an explicit argument, never the global \texttt{np.random} state, so output is fully determined by the seed. Third, package functions never print or plot; parameters, figures, and the saved \texttt{npz} summaries live in the phase notebooks. Fourth, each module implements one small, named slice of the model, so every equation of section~\ref{sec:model} traces to code; Table~\ref{tab:modules} gives the mapping. % src: CLAUDE.md module table (lines 54-61); module docstrings in src/reflexive_market/

\subsection{Phased construction}

The simulator was built in seven strictly ordered phases, a staging discipline fixed in the working agreement before any simulator code was written; phase $N$ does not start until phase $N-1$ runs end to end, meets its recorded done-when condition, and is committed. % src: proposal v4 section 5 staging table (verify-before-extend ordering); CLAUDE.md "Implementation phases" preamble (lines 65-67, run-end-to-end-and-commit gate); per-phase "Done when" blocks and "How to work on a phase" steps 3 and 6 (lines 256 and 259, done-when confirmation and next-phase gate)
The done-when criteria for phases 1 to 3 are unchanged since the scaffold; those for phases 4 to 7 were restated under the dual-channel framing after all seven phases had been implemented, once that framing had settled as the organising description of the result. % src: git diff 52b885a..HEAD -- CLAUDE.md (phase 1-3 "Done when" blocks unchanged; phase 4-7 blocks edited in v3-alignment commit 82ddb73, phase 7's as a dual-channel relabel of "the mechanism"; phase 4-6 blocks relabelled to demand-adjusted terms in c1778ca and phase 6 refined again in ba41aad; all postdating phase 7 commit 1a334cf)
The ordering enforces the design's central feasibility principle: the market must work before the forecasting rule is added, the rule before adoption, and extensions only once the baseline produces stable, interpretable results, so that any observed reflexivity effect traces to a small number of identifiable ingredients. % src: proposal v4 section 5, "most important feasibility principle" paragraph
Each phase owns one notebook (\texttt{notebooks/phase\_NN\_<name>.ipynb}) with every parameter in a single top cell, and saves its figures and \texttt{npz} summaries under \texttt{results/}. All seven phases are implemented; section 6 reports their results in build order. % src: notebooks/README.md phase status table ("All seven phases are implemented"); CLAUDE.md notebook conventions (parameters cell, save cell)

\begin{enumerate}[itemsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Baseline market.} Implement the single-asset market with every trader on the random null rule and confirm the empirical lag-1 autocorrelation of returns matches the input $\varphi$ (section~\ref{sec:phases12-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 1 definition
\item \textbf{Benchmark validation.} Rerun the null market across many seeds to verify stable descriptive statistics and a drift-free rolling effective AR coefficient, the no-adoption control behind every later erosion claim (section~\ref{sec:phases12-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 2 definition
\item \textbf{AR forecasting rule, offline.} Add rolling AR fitting and score the forecast against realised returns while no trader acts on it, recovering a small positive out-of-sample $R^{2}$ driven by the $\varphi r_t$ term (section~\ref{sec:phase3-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 3 definition (kept offline, as built; the proposal v4 section 5 table folds forecast-based trading with position caps into its phase 3 row, which the build defers to phase 4)
\item \textbf{Forecast-based trading and stochastic adoption.} Let adopters trade the shared forecast while its use spreads by stochastic diffusion; the dual-channel result first appears here (section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 4 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Performance-based adoption.} Replace diffusion with certainty-equivalent switching and compare the two adoption mechanisms on paired shocks (section~\ref{sec:phase5-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 5 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Experiments and thresholds.} Sweep $(\mu, \varphi, w, p)$ and locate the relative critical adoption shares $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{da},\mathrm{rel}}$ and $A^{*}_{\varphi,\mathrm{rel}}$, defined in section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star}, across the grid (section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 6 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\item \textbf{Evaluation and extension.} Summarise the findings under the dual-channel framing and add the transaction-cost extension, reading $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit}}$ against zero and against the null benchmark (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}). % src: CLAUDE.md phase 7 definition; notebooks/README.md phase table
\end{enumerate}

The phases group into three blocks: phases 1 to 3 establish the pre-adoption control (a market with no forecast trading, a forecast with no market feedback, each verified on its own), phases 4 and 5 switch the feedback on and establish the dual-channel mechanism under two different adoption processes, and phases 6 and 7 quantify the mechanism, across the parameter grid and then in economic terms, with phase 7 doubling as the evaluation summary of the build.

\subsection{AR forecast performance without adoption (phase 3)}\label{sec:phase3-results}

Phase 3 attaches the rolling AR(1) rule of section~\ref{sec:model-rules} to the null market, in offline mode: the forecast is estimated and scored, but no trader acts on it. A single run uses $N=200$, $T=8000$, $\mu=0.05$, $\sigma=0.01$, $\sigma_q=1.0$, and seed 43, with the input coefficient raised to $\varphi=0.25$ so the rule has a clearly detectable signal to recover. % src: notebooks/phase_03_ar_forecast.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['phi_input'], ['seed']
Each period the rule fits an AR(1) on the most recent $w \in \{50, 100, 250\}$ returns and issues a strictly out-of-sample one-step forecast, scored post hoc against realised returns by the rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ \eqref{eq:r2-realised} and by full-sample mean squared forecast error (MSFE). % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['ar_windows'], ['ar_order'], ['eval_window']; src/reflexive_market/metrics.py rolling_oos_r2(), msfe()
With zero adoption the realised and demand-adjusted returns differ only by unpredictable null-order impact, so this subsection reports realised-return performance only; the targets separate once adopters start trading in phase 4. % src: notebooks/phase_03_ar_forecast.ipynb title cell (cell 0), note on realised vs demand-adjusted return

The rule recovers the signal it was built for: the mean rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ is small, positive for every window, and rising with window length, $0.013$ for $w=50$, $0.033$ for $w=100$, and $0.047$ for $w=250$. % src: phase_03_ar_forecast.npz['summary'][:, 2]

\subsection{Dual-channel result under stochastic adoption (phase 4)}\label{sec:phase4-results}

Phase 4 turns the offline forecast into a tradeable rule and lets its use spread. Adopters map the shared rolling AR(1) forecast ($w=250$) into positions through the mean-variance demand \eqref{eq:bh-demand}, clipped per \eqref{eq:advanced-order}; non-adopters keep the null rule. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb parameters cell (cell 1); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['forecast_window']
With combined risk scaling $a\sigma^{2}=0.001$ and per-trader cap $\bar{q}=0.05$ the cap binds in nearly every period, so adopters are effectively fixed-size traders on the forecast's sign, with impact comparable in magnitude to the $\varphi r_t$ term rather than dominating it. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['risk_scale'], ['q_cap']; notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (comment on q_cap)
Adoption follows the stochastic diffusion of \eqref{eq:diffusion-entry} and \eqref{eq:diffusion-exit} in three regimes, zero adoption ($\pi=0$), slow diffusion ($\pi=3\times10^{-4}$), and fast diffusion ($\pi=10^{-3}$), each with exit probability $\delta=0$, on paired shocks from the shared seed 71 so that cross-regime differences are attributable to adoption alone. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['regime_names'], ['regime_pi'], ['regime_delta'], ['seed']
The market is otherwise the phase 3 configuration ($N=200$, $T=8000$, $\mu=0.05$, input $\varphi=0.25$, $\sigma=0.01$, $\sigma_q=1.0$), with every rolling metric on a trailing 1000-period window. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['N'], ['T'], ['mu'], ['phi_input'], ['eval_window']; notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (sigma_news, sigma_q)
Adoption is held at zero until period 1250, the first period with a complete metric window; the headline comparison contrasts a low-adoption window (periods 1250 to 2750) with a high-adoption window (the final 1500 periods), by which point the slow regime's mean adoption share is 0.836 and the fast regime's 0.994. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cells 1 and 4 (adoption_start_t = 1250, summary_span = 1500); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][:, 1]

Against realised returns, the forecast improves as adoption rises: in the high-adoption window the fast regime's rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ against $r_{t+1}$ is 0.190, versus 0.070 for the zero-adoption control on the same shocks (0.065 in the control's own late window), with the slow regime in between at 0.165. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 3] = 0.1904, [0, 2] = 0.0695, [0, 3] = 0.0653, [1, 3] = 0.1651
The control is the right low-adoption anchor: the fast regime's own early window already averages an adoption share of 0.505, with realised $R^{2}$ lifted to 0.094. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 0] = 0.5048, [2, 2] = 0.0942
The mechanism is the decomposition $r_{t+1}=\mu D_t + x_{t+1}$ of \eqref{eq:decomposition}: every adopter trades in the direction of the same public forecast, so coordinated demand pushes the realised return in the forecast direction, and the forecast scores partly against its own price impact. The top-left panel of Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-dual-mc} shows this is not a one-seed accident: pooled across 100 Monte Carlo seeds, mean realised-return $R^{2}$ rises monotonically in adoption share. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cells 21 and 23 (num_seeds_mc = 100, mc_base_seed = 1000); results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png

The same forecast on the same path is simultaneously losing the signal it was built to exploit: against the demand-adjusted return $x_{t+1}=r_{t+1}-\mu D_t$ of section~\ref{sec:model-decomposition} (the counterfactual, not the full regression residual), the rolling out-of-sample $R^{2}$ falls to 0.039 in the fast regime's high-adoption window, versus 0.082 under the control, with the slow regime at 0.047. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][2, 5] = 0.0386, [0, 4] = 0.0821, [1, 5] = 0.0471; definition per proposal sections 3.1.5 and 3.6 (eq. 7)
The independent predictive content that survives once self-fulfilment is removed thus weakens as adoption rises: in the top-right panel of Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-dual-mc} the Monte Carlo mean declines, the drop steepening at high shares. % src: results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png

The rolling effective AR coefficient of section~\ref{sec:metrics-phi} is the mechanical link between the two readings: it rises from 0.282 under the control, close to the input $\varphi=0.25$, to 0.447 in the fast regime's high-adoption window (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-dual-mc}, bottom panel). % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470, ['phi_input'] = 0.25
Adopter demand responds positively to recent returns through the forecast, so $\mu D_t$ reinforces the lagged-return component and measured persistence grows with adoption; the rolling AR fit absorbs this amplified coefficient and systematically over-predicts the demand-adjusted process, whose own AR coefficient stays at the input 0.25 by construction. That over-prediction is the demand-adjusted decline of the previous paragraph. % src: mechanism per proposal section 4.1.3 (effective AR coefficient diagnostic); notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 16 (markdown)
The control meanwhile moves far less across the same two windows (realised 0.070 to 0.065, demand-adjusted 0.082 to 0.068, effective coefficient 0.282 to 0.278), so the diffusion-regime movements are adoption effects, not time effects. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, :] = (0, 0, 0.0695, 0.0653, 0.0821, 0.0676, 0.2821, 0.2776)

This is the dual-channel result, the headline finding of the report: adoption raises realised-return predictive performance through the self-fulfilment channel while eroding the independent demand-adjusted signal, on the same path and from the same forecast; which reading a forecaster sees depends entirely on whether the evaluation target includes the forecaster's own price impact.
The separation is a steady state of the saturated market, not a transient of the adoption ramp: extending the fast-diffusion regime to $T=30000$ periods across 50 Monte Carlo seeds, roughly 25000 of them at full adoption, the readings hold their separated levels throughout, the cross-seed realised mean near 0.17 and the demand-adjusted mean near 0.02, with no drift back together (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-saturation}). % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cells 11 to 13 (T_long = 30000, n_seeds_mc_saturation = 50, mc_saturation_base_seed = 5000; "roughly 25000 periods at A = 1"); plateau levels recomputed deterministically from those parameters (cross-seed means over t in [10000, 30000): realised 0.170, demand-adjusted 0.022; mean adoption share first reaches 0.99 at t = 5836); results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_saturation_mc.png

\subsection{Endogenous CE-based switching (phase 5)}\label{sec:phase5-results}

Phase 5 makes adoption respond to how the forecast is actually paying: stochastic diffusion is replaced by the certainty-equivalent switching of section~\ref{sec:model-adoption}, scored on the realised one-period profit $q_{i,t}\, r_{t+1}$ of one representative trader per rule, the common capped adopter position and a representative non-adopter's order. % src: notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 0; src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() docstring (null_profit, advanced_profit, eq. 8)
Each rule's certainty equivalent \eqref{eq:ce} is computed over $W_A=500$ periods with $a=1$, and each non-adopter switches to the advanced rule with the logistic probability \eqref{eq:logit-switch} in the score gap $S_t=\mathrm{CE}^{(A)}_t-\mathrm{CE}^{(0)}_t$, with $\alpha=-5$ and $\beta=10000$. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_window'] = 500, ['switching_a'] = 1.0, ['switching_alpha'] = -5.0, ['switching_beta'] = 10000.0; proposal section 3.5, eqs. (14) and (15)
At a zero score the implied switch probability is $\Lambda(-5)\approx0.0067$ per period, and score movements of order $10^{-4}$ move it strongly in either direction. % src: computed from phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_alpha'] and ['switching_beta']: 1/(1+e^5) = 0.00669; beta = 1e4 makes a score of 1e-4 worth one logit
Everything else stays at the phase 4 configuration with adoption starting at period 1250; the comparator is the fast stochastic regime ($\pi=10^{-3}$) rerun on paired shocks from seed 71, so any difference is attributable to the adoption mechanism. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['stochastic_pi'] = 1e-3, ['seed'] = 71, ['T'], ['N'], ['mu'], ['phi_input'], ['eval_window']; notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (adoption_start_t = 1250)

\subsection{Parameter sweep and critical adoption shares (phase 6)}\label{sec:phase6-results}

Phase 6 turns the single-configuration findings of phases 4 and 5 into a map. The sweep crosses $\mu\in\{0.025, 0.05, 0.075, 0.10, 0.15\}$, $\varphi\in\{0.05,\dots,0.30\}$, windows $w\in\{100, 250, 500\}$, and AR orders $p\in\{1, 2, 5, 10\}$, AR(1) the baseline and higher orders a robustness extension, at 10 seeds per cell (3600 runs). % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['mu_grid'], ['phi_grid'], ['w_grid'], ['forecast_p_grid'], ['num_seeds']; notebooks/phase_06_experiments_threshold.ipynb cell 1

The zero-crossing threshold $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{da}}$ fires only where price impact is strong or the baseline signal is near zero; at the phase 4 and 5 settings the demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ falls but stays positive. The realised-return threshold is the designed non-result: $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{realised},\mathrm{rel}}$ is reached in only 28 of the 90 AR(1) cells, always early (mean share 0.06, maximum 0.21), with 26 of the 28 hits at $\varphi\leq0.20$ and a median baseline realised $R^{2}$ of 0.014 at the hit cells. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (28 finite; nanmean = 0.062; max = 0.208); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] at hit cells (median 0.014, 86 percent below 0.02; 26 of 28 hits at phi <= 0.20)
These are noise crossings, not erosion: where the baseline realised $R^{2}$ is near zero, the half-baseline threshold sits at essentially zero and rolling noise can dip below it without any systematic decline. The substantive realised-channel finding is the absence of crossings elsewhere: the tail-window realised $R^{2}$ rises with both $\varphi$ and $\mu$, up to 0.32 at the strongest cell, the self-fulfilment channel of phase 4 at grid scale. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (max 0.318 at w = 500, phi = 0.30, mu = 0.15; w = 250 slice spans 0.002 to 0.313, rising in phi and mu); proposal section 4.3 (A*_{R2,realised} typically not reached, the absence is a finding)
The same caveat covers the bottom rows of the demand-adjusted map: at $\varphi\leq0.10$ the demand-adjusted baseline is also near zero, and those rows fail the filter or fire at near-zero shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 and 0.10 row means -0.013 and -0.003); ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (phi = 0.10 crossings at 0.00 to 0.15)

The dual-channel pattern survives every AR order tested. At each $p$ the effective-coefficient threshold is reached on 80 to 82 of 90 cells at mean shares 0.38 to 0.41, the tail demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ deepens with $\mu$, and the realised threshold stays sparse (15 to 28 cells) and confined to low shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_phi'] by p (82, 82, 81, 80 finite; nanmeans 0.379 to 0.407); ['tail_R2_da_grid'] (mu-column means fall in mu at every p); ['a_star_R2_realised'] by p (28, 26, 23, 15 finite; nanmeans 0.062 to 0.084)
Across the full four-order grid the demand-adjusted threshold is reached in 196 of 360 cells (54 percent, per-seed hit rate 0.53) at a mean share of 0.27, against a realised-channel hit rate of 0.26. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'] (196 of 360 finite; nanmean = 0.273); ['hit_rate_R2_da'].mean() = 0.530; ['hit_rate_R2_realised'].mean() = 0.256
Reachability falls with order (67, 59, 42, 28 of 90 cells for $p=1, 2, 5, 10$) because the baseline falls with it (grid means 0.035, 0.028, 0.004, $-0.041$): a higher-order rule fits more coefficients on the same window, starts with less out-of-sample signal, and more often fails the positive-baseline requirement or the hit-rate filter. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'] by p; ['baseline_R2_da_grid'] by p (grid nanmeans)
Where both AR(1) and AR($p$) set a value, the delta-from-AR(1) map (Appendix B) is mildly negative on average ($-0.04$ at $p=2$, $-0.14$ at $p=5$, $-0.20$ at $p=10$): higher-order rules cross somewhat earlier, consistent with having less signal to lose, and nowhere does the mechanism reverse. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'] minus its AR(1) slice (mean deltas over jointly finite cells = -0.036, -0.135, -0.204); results/figures/phase_06_delta_a_star_r2_from_ar1.png
The erosion of the independent signal is therefore a property of the mechanism, not of the AR(1) specification.

\subsection{Transaction-cost extension and economic endpoint (phase 7)}\label{sec:phase7-results}

Phase 7 closes the results with the transaction-cost extension and the economic endpoint it enables. A proportional cost $c$ is charged on every unit traded, so per-period adopter net profit is $q^{(A)}_t r_{t+1} - c\,\lvert q^{(A)}_t\rvert$; the dynamics are unchanged and costs enter only the profit accounting, so 50 paired-shock seed runs of the phase 4 slow-diffusion design support the whole sweep over $c\in\{0, 0.001, 0.002, 0.003, 0.004, 0.005, 0.0075, 0.01\}$. % src: notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb parameters cell (costs enter only the P&L accounting); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['c_grid'], ['num_seeds'] = 50, ['adoption_pi'] = 3e-4
Net profit is pooled across seeds and time into 30 adoption-share bins; each cost level reports the $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit}}$ of section~\ref{sec:metrics-a-star} in both conventions, $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,abs}}$ where mean absolute net profit crosses zero and $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,rel}}$ where mean net profit relative to the null benchmark does. % src: proposal section 4.3 (A*_profit reported separately for the null-relative primary and absolute secondary conventions); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb first_zero_crossing cell
The benchmark, the null leg of the primary economic endpoint \eqref{eq:null-relative-profit}, is the representative null trader's realised gross profit minus its own cost bill $c\,E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert$, with $E\lvert q^{(0)}\rvert=\sqrt{2/\pi}\,\sigma_q\approx0.80$. % src: proposal section 4.1.4 (net profit relative to the null benchmark, primary); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] = 0.7979

At zero cost the rule pays at every observed share and pays more as adoption grows, from a pooled mean of $1.2\times10^{-4}$ per period at $A<0.2$ to $2.0\times10^{-4}$ at $A>0.6$. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'][0, 1] = 1.19e-4, [0, 2] = 2.02e-4
That growth, roughly $0.8\times10^{-4}$ between the two windows, recurs at every cost level: cost shifts the absolute curves down in parallel and leaves the slope in adoption untouched (Figure~\ref{fig:phase07-net-profit}, left). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 minus column 1 = 8.3e-5 in every row)
The absolute threshold therefore exists only in a narrow band: the binned curve is positive at every observed share for $c\leq0.002$, negative everywhere for $c\geq0.005$, and crosses zero only at $c=0.003$ and $0.004$, at $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,abs}}=0.35$ and $0.72$ (Figure~\ref{fig:phase07-a-star-profit}). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['a_star_profit_abs'] = (nan, nan, nan, 0.35, 0.717, nan, nan, nan); ['binned_abs_means'] (all 28 populated bins positive at c <= 0.002, all negative at c >= 0.005)
The crossing runs from below, negative at low adoption and positive at high: a profitability onset, the share self-fulfilment needs before the rule covers its costs, the reverse of the from-above erosion reading the threshold was designed to detect. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'][3, 1:3] = (-3.0e-5, +5.3e-5), [4, 1:3] = (-8.0e-5, +0.3e-5); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb direction note above the first_zero_crossing cell
The null-relative curve produces no meaningful crossing. At $c=0$ it is slightly negative in 26 of the 28 populated bins and at both pooled ends ($-2.0\times10^{-4}$ at $A<0.2$, $-0.5\times10^{-4}$ at $A>0.6$); at every positive cost level it is uniformly positive. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['binned_rel_means'][0] (26 of 28 bins with counts > 10 are negative), ['summary'][0, 3] = -1.96e-4, [0, 4] = -0.53e-4; ['binned_rel_means'][1:] (every populated bin positive at every c >= 0.001)
The single detected crossing, $A^{*}_{\mathrm{profit,rel}}\approx0.68$ at $c=0$, is one bin popping to $+1.7\times10^{-4}$ against an in-bin standard deviation of $0.011$: noise around a systematically negative mean, not a threshold. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['a_star_profit_rel'][0] = 0.683; ['binned_rel_means'][0] (bin 0.683 = +1.71e-4, bin 0.717 = -0.76e-4); ['binned_rel_stds'][0] (0.0109 at that bin)

The null-relative endpoint is therefore implemented exactly as designed but dominated by position-size asymmetry in both directions, and the absolute endpoint is the cleaner economic read in this baseline: adopter mean profit grows with adoption at every cost level tested, transaction costs setting only the level at which the growth starts to pay. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 exceeds column 1 in every row); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb done-when cell

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpretation of the dual-channel mechanism}\label{sec:discussion-interpretation}

The dual-channel result of section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} is one mechanism seen through two evaluation targets, not two findings that happen to coexist. Adoption makes the forecast self-fulfilling against the target a deployed forecaster observes: coordinated adopter demand moves the price in the forecast's direction, so the scored realised return becomes partly a product of the forecast itself (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-dual-mc}, top-left panel). % src: restates sec:phase4-results, mechanism per the decomposition \eqref{eq:decomposition}; results/figures/phase_04_oos_r2_vs_adoption_mc.png (left panel is the realised target)
Against the demand-adjusted target, the counterfactual of \eqref{eq:decomposition} with contemporaneous price impact removed, the same adoption is purely degrading: the rolling fit calibrates to the amplified persistence of realised returns (bottom panel) and systematically over-predicts a process whose persistence has not changed. % src: restates sec:phase4-results (demand-adjusted decline, effective-coefficient mechanism); results/figures/phase_04_phi_vs_adoption_mc.png
Self-fulfilment and erosion are two faces of the same coordinated order flow, so they arrive together on the same paths and persist across the sweep of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}.

The practical lesson for a deployed forecaster is that realised-return $R^{2}$ over-reports the forecasting signal once the forecast has users with price impact. The realised reading conflates the stable signal the rule was trained to recover, the $\varphi r_t$ persistence of \eqref{eq:return-law}, with the price-impact channel created by its own adopters; as adoption grows, the headline number improves exactly while the independent content it appears to certify erodes. The top panel of Figure~\ref{fig:phase06-thresholds} maps the shares at which that content has lost half of its own low-adoption baseline. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5 (half-baseline convention per sec:metrics-a-star); results/figures/phase_06_a_star_R2_heatmap.png
Realised evaluation therefore cannot distinguish a forecast that predicts the market from one the market has come to obey; the demand-adjusted audit that separates them requires exactly what an external evaluator usually lacks, the period's aggregate order flow $D_t$ and the impact coefficient $\mu$. % src: x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - mu D_t per \eqref{eq:decomposition}; inputs are D_t and mu, observable inside the simulator but not to an outside evaluator

For theory, the result bears on the performative-prediction view of deployed models, in which measured accuracy depends on the behaviour the prediction induces and is commonly treated as splitting into a stable and a deployment-driven component. The simulator makes that split measurable in a market with price impact: the demand-adjusted target identifies the stable component, the gap between the two readings measures the deployment-driven one, and the threshold maps of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results} chart the adoption needed to move each diagnostic by a fixed fraction of its baseline. % src: positioning claim only, citations deferred to the Background section; phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['r2_threshold_factor'] = 0.5, ['phi_threshold_factor'] = 1.5 (the fixed fractions)

\subsection{Why the effective AR coefficient rises with adoption}\label{sec:discussion-phi}

The rise in measured persistence in section~\ref{sec:phase4-results}, from 0.282 under the zero-adoption control to 0.447 in the fast regime's high-adoption window, follows mechanically from three chained ingredients of section~\ref{sec:model}. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470
First, at $p=1$ the shared forecast \eqref{eq:ar-forecast} is affine in the latest return, with an estimated slope tracking the fitting window's lag-1 autocorrelation, noisy but centred well above zero in these runs, so the forecast typically carries the sign of $r_t$ when the lagged term outweighs the small intercept. % src: src/reflexive_market/forecast.py fit_ar (OLS slope on the lagged design); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][:, 6:8] (rolling lag-1 autocorrelation between 0.278 and 0.447 in every reported window); results/figures/phase_03_ar_coefficients.png (w = 250 slope estimate fluctuates around the input 0.25 with occasional dips toward zero, hence "noisy but centred well above zero" and "typically")
Second, adopters turn the forecast into demand, though not in proportion to its magnitude: the cap in \eqref{eq:advanced-order} binds in nearly every period at the phase 4 settings, so each adopter trades a fixed size $\bar{q}$ in the forecast's direction and the adopter block adds $A_t\,\bar{q}$, signed typically by $r_t$, to aggregate demand \eqref{eq:aggregate-demand}; sign-following rather than proportional demand changes the strength of what follows, not its direction. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 ("the clip binds in nearly every period"); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['q_cap'] = 0.05, ['risk_scale'] = 0.001; adopter contribution A_t q^(A) per src/reflexive_market/simulate.py run() (orders = (1 - adoption) * null + adoption * q_adv, averaged in \eqref{eq:aggregate-demand})
Third, the impact term $\mu D_t$ enters the return law \eqref{eq:return-law} with a positive sign, so the coordinated block pushes $r_{t+1}$ in the direction of $r_t$. % src: \eqref{eq:return-law} (mu > 0 per sec:model-market)

A next-return component that co-moves with the current one is, by construction, extra lag-1 autocorrelation in realised returns, exactly what the diagnostic of section~\ref{sec:metrics-phi} computes. Its amplitude grows with the adopter mass, of order $\mu A_t \bar{q}$, so the measured coefficient climbs steadily with adoption rather than jumping (Figure~\ref{fig:phase04-dual-mc}, bottom panel). % src: results/figures/phase_04_phi_vs_adoption_mc.png (smooth monotone rise across adoption-share bins, roughly 0.25 to 0.42)
A naive reading predicts the opposite, that market-maker absorption of order flow should damp persistence; it fails because $\mu D_t$ adds to the next-period return rather than offsetting it. % src: proposal v4 section 4.1.3 ("A naive reading ... does not survive once the sign of the price-impact term is tracked carefully")
Being structural, the rise appears under both adoption schemes at matched shares (section~\ref{sec:phase5-results}) and across the grid of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}. % src: phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['summary'] (phi 0.320 to 0.447 stochastic, 0.315 to 0.449 switching, as reported in sec:phase5-results); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_phi'] (A*_{phi,rel} populated across the grid)

\subsection{Boundary conditions and the realised-channel non-result}\label{sec:discussion-boundary}

The realised-return channel shows no systematic erosion in any regime tested, an outcome the design anticipated with a dedicated threshold: $A^{*}_{R^{2},\mathrm{realised},\mathrm{rel}}$ is reached in only 28 of the 90 AR(1) cells, against 67 for the demand-adjusted threshold, with per-seed hit rates of 0.26 versus 0.53 over the full four-order grid (section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}). % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (28 of 90 finite), ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (67 of 90 finite), ['hit_rate_R2_realised'].mean() = 0.256, ['hit_rate_R2_da'].mean() = 0.530
The hits sit at very low adoption, 23 of the 28 below $A=0.10$, and in rows where the baseline realised $R^{2}$ is small: 26 of the 28 are at $\varphi\leq0.20$, with a median baseline of 0.014 at the hit cells. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (23 of 28 finite values below 0.10; hits by phi row = 1, 8, 10, 7, 2, 0, so 26 of 28 at phi <= 0.20); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] at hit cells (median 0.014)
They are the noise crossings of section~\ref{sec:phase6-results}: a near-zero baseline puts the half-baseline threshold at essentially zero, within reach of rolling noise.

The grid also bounds each positive claim: the demand-adjusted erosion, the core finding, holds in 67 of 90 AR(1) cells and 196 of 360 over all four AR orders, deepens with $\mu$ throughout, and is robust to adoption mechanism and AR order across paired-shock seeds (sections~\ref{sec:phase5-results} and \ref{sec:phase6-results}). % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_da'][0] (67 of 90 finite) and full array (196 of 360 finite); ['tail_R2_da_grid'][0] (mu-column means fall 0.021, 0.007, -0.015, -0.048, -0.152); robustness per sec:phase5-results and sec:phase6-results
The realised-channel amplification claim belongs to the high-$\mu$, high-$\varphi$ corner: at $\varphi\geq0.25$ and $\mu\geq0.10$ the tail-window realised $R^{2}$ reaches 0.19 to 0.32, exceeding its own baseline by 0.15 to 0.24. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi >= 0.25, mu >= 0.10 block spans 0.191 to 0.318) minus ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] (lift spans 0.152 to 0.240 on that block)
Outside that corner the amplification weakens: the tail realised $R^{2}$ remains clearly positive at the section~\ref{sec:phase4-results} setting ($\varphi=0.25$, $\mu=0.05$) but flat, between $-0.01$ and $0.04$, over the $\varphi\leq0.10$, $\mu\leq0.05$ block and the entire $\varphi=0.05$ row at every $\mu$ tested: with no signal to amplify (baseline realised $R^{2}$ of $-0.009$), the channel never activates even at the strongest price impact. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['tail_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi = 0.25, mu = 0.05 cells span 0.119 to 0.131, lift about 0.075 over ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0]; phi <= 0.10, mu <= 0.05 block spans -0.006 to 0.040; phi = 0.05 row spans -0.006 to 0.027 at every mu, per-mu means over w = 0.0001, 0.014, 0.015, 0.017, 0.010; further near-flat cells outside the block: phi = 0.15, mu = 0.025 at w = 100 and 250 (0.028, 0.036; 0.042 at w = 500) and phi = 0.10, mu = 0.15, w = 100 (0.039), so 12 out-of-block cells sit at or below 0.04); ['baseline_R2_realised_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 row mean -0.009)
The lowest-$\varphi$ rows bound the erosion claim from below: at $\varphi\leq0.10$ the demand-adjusted baseline is near zero, leaving no signal to lose: the $\varphi=0.05$ row never crosses and the $\varphi=0.10$ row fires only at low shares. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['baseline_R2_da_grid'][0] (phi = 0.05 and 0.10 row means -0.013 and -0.003); ['a_star_R2_da'][0] (phi = 0.05 row all NaN; phi = 0.10 crossings 0.004 to 0.146); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb summary report cell (weak-signal boundary condition)
The boundary does not extend to the economic endpoint: adopter mean profit grows with adoption at every cost level tested (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}), so the erosion result concerns the independent signal, not profitability in this baseline. % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (column 2 exceeds column 1 in every row); notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb summary report cell (cost-free baseline shows no economic erosion)

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:limitations}

The findings carry two kinds of boundary: scope restrictions built into the minimal design, and implementation conventions that sections~\ref{sec:phase7-results}, \ref{sec:discussion-phi}, and \ref{sec:discussion-boundary} lean on directly.

\begin{itemize}
\item The market is single-asset, and linear price impact $\mu D_t$ is the only channel from trading back into returns; cross-asset and portfolio effects are out of scope, and non-linear impact, such as a square-root law, is untested. % src: design scope per sec:model-market (linear impact the single feedback channel; single risky asset); CLAUDE.md "Do not add multiple assets"
\item The only forecasting rule is the rolling AR($p$); machine-learning and non-parametric forecasters are excluded by design. Time is discrete, so latency and other microstructure effects are not modelled. % src: sec:model-rules (penalised regressions, tree ensembles, recurrent architectures deliberately excluded); sec:model-market (discrete time)
\item Non-adopters are a homogeneous pool of random null traders; a heterogeneous ecology of trend followers and contrarians remains an unimplemented extension. Transaction costs are linear and symmetric, with no inventory or financing costs. % src: sec:model-rules (null rule); proposal v4 extensions list ("Heterogeneous trader ecology ... trend followers, contrarians, and fundamentalists") and CLAUDE.md ("A second optional extension is heterogeneous trader ecology ... if time allows"; only the transaction-cost extension was implemented in phase 7); sec:phase7-results (proportional cost c only)
\item Profits are marked at the post-impact quote and unwinds never re-enter the order flow, so every profit figure is a paper profit under the model's own accounting; in particular the null benchmark books own-impact profit $\mu\sigma_q^{2}/N = 2.5\times10^{-4}$ per period on a position roughly 16 times the adopter cap, dominating the null-relative endpoint at $c=0$ (section~\ref{sec:phase7-results}). % src: sec:phase7-results; notebooks/phase_07_evaluation_extensions.ipynb closing accounting note and null-benchmark cell; phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['mu'] = 0.05, ['N'] = 200, sigma_q = 1.0 give 2.5e-4; ['null_mean_abs'] / ['q_cap'] = 15.96
\item The market maker absorbs position levels rather than position changes, the \citet{beja1980} convention rather than \citet{farmerjoshi2002} order flow, so a persistent position exerts persistent price pressure, strengthening the persistence mechanism of section~\ref{sec:discussion-phi}. % src: src/reflexive_market/market.py module docstring; sec:model-market implementation note
\item At the baseline risk scale and position cap the clip in \eqref{eq:advanced-order} binds in nearly every period, so adopters trade as fixed-size sign followers rather than graded mean-variance investors. Relatedly, the switching rule's risk-aversion coefficient ($a=1$) is set independently of the roughly 7 implied by the decision layer's risk scale at the realised return variance, keeping the certainty-equivalent penalty mild. % src: notebooks/phase_04_stochastic_adoption.ipynb cell 1 ("the clip binds in nearly every period"); phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['risk_scale'] = 0.001, ['q_cap'] = 0.05; phase_05_performance_adoption.npz['switching_a'] = 1.0; notebooks/phase_05_performance_adoption.ipynb cell 1 (switching_a "set independently of risk_scale (which implies a ~ 7 at the realised return variance), so the penalty stays mild")
\end{itemize}


\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{Summary of findings}

The headline finding is the dual-channel result: adoption of the shared rolling AR(1) forecast lifts its realised-return out-of-sample $R^{2}$ from 0.07 under a zero-adoption control to 0.19 at near-full adoption, while on the same path its demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ falls from 0.08 to 0.04 and measured persistence rises from 0.28 to 0.45. % src: phase_04_stochastic_adoption.npz['summary'][0, 2] = 0.0695, [2, 3] = 0.1904 (realised); [0, 4] = 0.0821, [2, 5] = 0.0386 (demand-adjusted); [0, 6] = 0.2821, [2, 7] = 0.4470 (effective AR coefficient); matches the abstract and sec:phase4-results
One coordinated order flow drives both channels, and the pattern survives endogenous switching and a 3600-run sweep over price impact, input persistence, estimation window, and AR order. % src: sec:phase5-results (matched erosion under CE switching); phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['mu_grid'] (5) x ['phi_grid'] (6) x ['w_grid'] (3) x ['forecast_p_grid'] (4) x ['num_seeds'] (10) = 3600 runs per sec:phase6-results
Economically, adoption amplifies rather than erodes adopter profit: the mean grows with adoption at every transaction-cost level tested, costs setting only the share at which the growth starts to pay (0.35 and 0.72 at $c=0.003$ and $0.004$). % src: phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['summary'] (high-adoption column exceeds low-adoption column in every cost row); ['a_star_profit_abs'] = (nan, nan, nan, 0.35, 0.717, nan, nan, nan), per sec:phase7-results
The null-relative comparison is dominated by position-size asymmetry and carries no clean forecast-content reading. % src: sec:phase7-results (asymmetry between the null position E|q0| = 0.798 and the adopter cap 0.05); phase_07_transaction_costs.npz['null_mean_abs'] / ['q_cap'] = 15.96; null own-impact profit mu sigma_q^2 / N = 2.5e-4
The realised-return channel itself never erodes systematically: its threshold fires in only 28 of 90 AR(1) cells, all weak-signal noise crossings at near-zero baselines. % src: phase_06_a_star_grid.npz['a_star_R2_realised'][0] (28 of 90 finite); noise-crossing reading per sec:discussion-boundary (median baseline 0.014 at the hit cells, 26 of 28 at phi <= 0.20)

\subsection{Implications for forecasting under adoption}

The practical lesson is about measurement. Realised-return $R^{2}$, the only score a deployed forecaster can compute, over-reports the forecast once it has users with price impact: it conflates the stable signal the rule was estimated to capture with the price impact of the rule's own adopters, and here it improves exactly while the independent content erodes. % src: restates sec:discussion-interpretation (realised reading conflates phi r_t persistence with the mu D_t channel); sec:phase4-results (realised rises while demand-adjusted falls on the same path)
The demand-adjusted $R^{2}$ is the cleaner signal of whether the underlying forecasting hypothesis still holds, since it scores the forecast against a target with contemporaneous price impact removed; computing it requires the period's aggregate order flow and the impact coefficient, exactly what an external evaluator typically lacks. % src: x_{t+1} = r_{t+1} - mu D_t per \eqref{eq:decomposition} (counterfactual stripping contemporaneous impact only, not the full regression residual); inputs D_t and mu per sec:discussion-interpretation
Where adoption is plausible, both readings should therefore be reported together: their gap measures the deployment-driven component of measured accuracy, the quantity the realised number silently absorbs. % src: dual reporting per sec:model-decomposition (both readings reported on the same path); gap-as-deployment-component framing per sec:discussion-interpretation

\subsection{Directions for future work}

Each direction for future work relaxes a limitation of section~\ref{sec:limitations}. Non-linear price impact, such as a square-root law, would test the mechanism against realistic impact concavity, and a heterogeneous ecology of trend followers and contrarians would set other strategies against the coordinated adopters. % src: sec:limitations items 1 and 3 (square-root impact untested; heterogeneous ecology an unimplemented extension per CLAUDE.md)
Machine-learning forecasters and multi-asset extensions would ask whether richer rules and cross-asset demand reproduce, dampen, or redirect the same two channels. % src: sec:limitations items 1 and 2 (single-asset scope; ML and non-parametric forecasters excluded by design)
